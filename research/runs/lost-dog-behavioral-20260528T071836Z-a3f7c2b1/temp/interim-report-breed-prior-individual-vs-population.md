# Interim report: breed-prior-individual-vs-population

**Locus question:** Does breed predict evasion/flight behavior well enough for Nona's intake scoring? Morrill 2022 shows agonistic threshold is 'almost uninformative' for breed at individual level; Normando 2024 shows galgo stranger-fear significantly elevated at group level (p=0.009, n=410). Which analytical level should govern Nona's breed prior weighting?
**Flavor:** dialectical

## What the corpus already said

Before this investigation, the corpus contained several interlocking sources that set up the dialectic. [[ancestry-inclusive-dog-genomics-challenges-popular-breed-stereotypes-pmc]] (Morrill et al. 2022, Science, N=18,385) established the core anti-breed-prior argument: breed explains just 9% of individual behavioral variation, and agonistic threshold — the fear/reactivity dimension most directly governing xenophobic lost-dog behavior — is "almost uninformative" by breed. The source analyst's digest [[source-analysis-morrill-et-al-2022-ancestry-inclusive-dog-genomics]] extracted the key numbers: h²SNP for agonistic threshold is at the low end across all factors; no breed-specific genetic effect on this trait was confirmed in the mixed-breed ancestry analysis. On the other side, [[prevalence-of-some-behavioural-problems-in-two-groups-of-rescue-dogs-galgos-from]] (Normando et al. 2024, n=410) showed galgo stranger-fear elevated at population level (p=0.009), and [[behavioural-characteristics-of-sighthounds-an-exploratory-investigation-dog-beha]] (Normando et al. 2025, n=768 C-BARQ) confirmed within-sighthound predatory and attachment differences between breeds (p<0.001). The contradiction graph in the vault had already named this as the key fight: individual-level prediction (Morrill wins) vs. population-level group mean difference (Normando wins). [[holding-back-the-genes-limitations-of-research-into-canine-behavioural-genetics]] (van Rooy et al. 2014) provided methodological grounding: breed-level mean differences are real and measurable in large-N studies, but within-breed variance always dominates. [[about-the-c-barq-c-barq-canine-behavioral-assessment-research-questionnaire]] established the C-BARQ as the correct instrument for individual-level behavioral scoring, superior to breed-based assumptions.

## What the new sources say

The McLennan podenco prey-drive study ([[prey-drive-in-podencos]]) and the Greyhound Pets Inc. resource ([[lost-dog-resources-greyhound-pets-inc]]) provided breed-type behavioral specifics from practitioner and published-research sources. The consensus claims file provided a pivotal data point: the vault already contains a "strong agreement" claim (5 independent sources) that "Galgo español require weeks to months of passive, patient coaxing before any human can approach them — immediate direct approach reliably fails," encoding a rule: `BREED_PRIOR[galgo]: stranger_approach_success_rate_day1 = 0.02`. The podenco consensus claim (4 independent sources) encodes: `BREED_PRIOR[podenco]: predatory_drive = very_high; Consume = 8%; food_lure_effectiveness_during_chase = 0.1`. These practitioner-derived priors converge with the Normando 2024 group-level finding on a species of Bayesian question Morrill 2022 does not address: not "does breed predict individual agonistic threshold?" but "what is the optimal prior distribution over stranger-approach behavior for a breed group, given no individual assessment data?"

**[[prey-drive-in-podencos]] (McLennan, Applied Animal Behaviour Science):** Research study percentages for Podencos — Hunt: 69%, Orient: 69%, Eye: 54%, Stalk: 23%, Chase: 69%, Grab-bite: 69%, Possess: 31%, Kill-bite: 46%, Dissect: 23%, Consume: 8%. The Consume figure is critical: 8% means podencos hunt intrinsically, not for food reward, making food-lure effectiveness near-zero during active chase. This is a breed-type measurable prior for capture strategy, not for stranger-fear prediction specifically.

**[[lost-dog-resources-greyhound-pets-inc]] (Greyhound Pets Inc., practitioner):** "Do not approach a shy/loose dog — instead open car door and invite, or run away to trigger prey-chase instinct." This practitioner encoding of sighthound-specific approach failure is consistent with Normando 2024's finding that >1 in 5 galgos show out-of-context stranger fear (p=0.009). The greyhound rescue community operationalizes the group-level behavioral prior: assume flight-from-strangers as the default, use passive and conspecific capture methods.

**[[about-the-c-barq-c-barq-canine-behavioral-assessment-research-questionnaire]] (C-BARQ, Hsu & Serpell 2003, Penn Vet):** "Individual dog C-BARQ score is compared to population averages from thousands of dogs — this is the correct architecture for individual temperament assessment over breed priors." C-BARQ's 14 factors include "stranger-directed fear" as a distinct subscale. The mini C-BARQ (Duffy, Kruger & Serpell 2015) was designed specifically for shelter intake screening. This is the institutional answer to the locus question from the psychometric measurement side: use a standardized individual assessment instrument, not breed ancestry, for individual prediction.

**MacLean et al. 2019 (PNAS, cited in Morrill 2022 and van Rooy 2014 reference list):** This paper — "Highly heritable and functionally relevant breed differences in dog behaviour" — represents the strongest pro-breed-prior position in the academic literature. It cannot be accessed directly in this investigation (403 blocked), but its claims are documented via the Morrill 2022 citation context: MacLean et al. argue breed differences in behavior are "highly heritable and functionally relevant" using C-BARQ data across breeds, finding substantial between-breed variation. Crucially, the Morrill paper — which is a 2022 Science paper with N=18,385 — explicitly positions itself as a correction to MacLean-style breed-level analyses, noting that breed-level analyses conflate genetic and cultural/stereotyping effects, which mixed-breed admixture analysis can partially disentangle.

## Evidence synthesis

### Position A: Breed is uninformative for individual evasion prediction (Morrill 2022)

The strongest evidence for this position comes from Morrill et al. 2022 (Science, N=18,385, genome-sequenced N=2,155): "For less heritable, less breed-differentiated traits, like agonistic threshold (factor 5), which measures how easily a dog is provoked by frightening, uncomfortable, or annoying stimuli, breed is almost uninformative." The mechanism is explicit: behavioral GWAS loci are NOT unusually differentiated across breeds (PBS z = −0.001, p=0.224), meaning Victorian-era breed formation did not selectively fix behavioral variants. Modern breeds are ~160 years old (~50–80 generations), with only 332 of 16.7 million SNPs exclusive to and fixed within any breed (0.002%). The overall breed effect on behavioral variation is 9±3% (marginal R² across behavioral factor scores in admixed dogs). Within every breed of ≥25 dogs, 67.2±7.5% score within 1 SD of the population mean on any factor.

The individual-level implication for Nona is direct: if a galgo's breed classification is entered as the only prior for evasion behavior, the model is working from a prior that explains at most 9% of the variance in the relevant behavioral dimension, and agonistic threshold — the core of stranger-fear/flight behavior — is explicitly the dimension where breed contributes least. Knowing a dog is a galgo does not tell you whether it will flee from rescuers vs. approach; the within-breed variance swamps the between-breed signal at the individual level.

Van Rooy et al. 2014 reinforces this structurally: "within-breed variance is always high" even for traits where between-breed means differ significantly. The Swedish DMA study cited by van Rooy (13,000+ dogs, 31 breeds) found statistically significant between-breed aggression score differences AND high within-breed variance simultaneously — both things are true.

### Position B: Breed-group means are real and encode valid population-level priors (Normando 2024/2025, practitioner consensus)

The strongest evidence for this position is Normando et al. 2024 (Dog Behavior, n=410 rescue dogs — 198 galgos vs. 212 other dogs): galgos show significantly higher prevalence of "out of context fear of non-cohabiting adult people" (p=0.009), with >1 in 5 galgos exhibiting this behavior vs. effectively zero in the control group. This is not a marginal result — it is a discrete behavioral difference that translates directly into a lost-dog search strategy. If the prior probability that a lost galgo will approach a stranger is substantially lower than for a non-galgo, the search strategy must adapt: no direct approach, conspecific lure, trap deployment from Day 1.

The vault's consensus claims (5 independent practitioner sources) encode this prior directly: galgo stranger_approach_success_rate_day1 = 0.02. This is not the same claim as "galgo individual agonistic threshold is predictable from breed." It is the claim that the group-level distribution is shifted enough that the base rate justifies a different default search protocol.

Normando et al. 2025 (n=768, C-BARQ sections) adds that predatory behavior differences across sighthound breeds are statistically significant (p<0.001): Saluki/Cirneco highest, Piccolo Levriero Italiano lowest. Within sighthounds, breed sub-type further modulates predatory drive. This is a within-type breed effect — not captured by Morrill's analysis, which does not disaggregate sighthounds specifically.

McLennan's podenco prey-drive data (Applied Animal Behaviour Science) provides a quantified breed-type prior for a different but equally important behavioral dimension: Consume = 8% means the predatory motor sequence is intrinsically motivated, not food-terminable. This directly governs capture strategy (food lures work only for hungry dogs, not dogs in pursuit).

Position B does not claim that individual breed prediction is reliable. It claims that the group-level distribution differs enough to shift the optimal prior in a Bayesian intake model — which is a much weaker and more defensible claim.

## Committed position

Nona should use breed as a **weak, degradable prior** in its intake scoring, applied at the level of behavioral strategy defaults (capture method, approach protocol, trap deployment timing) rather than as a continuous weight in the stranger-fear probability score. The Morrill/Normando tension is real but is not an irreconcilable contradiction: it is a scope mismatch. Morrill 2022 proves that individual agonistic threshold is not reliably predictable from breed ancestry — this is the correct analytical frame for any claim like "this galgo will be afraid of this rescuer." Normando 2024 proves that the galgo group-level distribution of stranger-fear is significantly shifted vs. the control group (p=0.009, n=410) — this is the correct analytical frame for "what default search protocol should Nona assign before any individual signals are collected?" The resolution for Nona's engine is to use breed priors only to set the starting distribution over *capture-strategy type*, not to score individual evasion probability. A lost galgo enters the engine with default_protocol = passive_conspecific_trap (because ≥20% prevalence of complete stranger-avoidance at the group level justifies this as the minimum-regret choice), while the stranger-fear probability score itself starts at the population base rate (25–30%) and is updated by individual signals: history with strangers, time lost, territory familiarity, whether the dog has been pursued. The breed classification shifts the strategy prior, not the behavioral probability estimate. Any individual signals collected within 24 hours — owner-reported stranger behavior, shelter history, observed body language — should override the breed prior entirely for the probability estimate, because 91% of behavioral variance is explained by factors other than breed ancestry.

- **Position:** Breed classification should govern capture-strategy defaults in Nona (galgo → passive/conspecific protocol from Day 1; podenco → trap + scent trail, not food-lure-during-chase), but should contribute ≤10% weight to the probabilistic stranger-fear score, with individual behavioral signals dominating from the first data point collected.
- **Confidence:** high (>80%) — the scope distinction is directly supported by Morrill 2022's explicit statement ("breed is almost uninformative" for agonistic threshold), Normando 2024's significant group-level difference (p=0.009), and the logical structure that population priors justify strategy defaults while individual variance governs probability estimates.
- **Boundary conditions:** This position applies to (a) purebred or visually identified galgos and podencos in the Algarve rescue context, where breed provenance is relatively reliable (rescued from hunting kennels with known breed history); (b) the intake stage, before any individual behavioral signals have been collected. For mixed-breed dogs of unknown ancestry (the majority of the world's dog population and a substantial fraction of Algarve dogs), breed priors collapse to population base rates. For any dog where the owner has provided behavioral history with strangers, that history overrides breed classification completely. The podenco Consume=8% prior is the most robust because it is an intrinsic motivational fact (prey drive does not terminate on food) rather than a probabilistic fear estimate.
- **What would change this position:** A large-N (N>500) randomized study comparing rescue outcomes (days-to-capture) for galgo-default-passive-protocol vs. galgo-active-approach-protocol in real lost-dog cases would directly test whether the group-level prior translates into strategy effectiveness. If such a study found no significant days-to-capture difference between protocols, the breed-prior for strategy selection would lose its justification. Separately, if Morrill 2022's finding were replicated specifically in sighthound breeds with a sighthound-oversampled cohort (Morrill's dataset is 85% US pet dogs, with galgos/podencos essentially absent), and agonistic threshold still showed breed = uninformative within that group, the strategy-prior justification would weaken considerably. The key Achilles' heel is that Normando 2024 is a convenience sample (n=198 galgos) using owner-report data with potential stereotype inflation — exactly the methodological concern Morrill 2022 flags for owner-reported breed behavioral profiles in general.
- **Evidence weight:** 2 empirical studies directly support individual-level unpredictability (Morrill 2022 Science N=18,385; van Rooy 2014 review). 2 empirical studies support population-level group differences (Normando 2024 n=410 p=0.009; Normando 2025 n=768 p<0.001). 1 quantified practitioner dataset supports breed-type capture-strategy encoding (McLennan podenco prey-drive, Applied Animal Behaviour Science). 5 independent practitioner sources (consensus claim, strong agreement) corroborate galgo stranger_approach_success_rate_day1 ≈ 0.02. The critical gap: no published study has tested whether breed-specific capture protocols yield better outcomes than generic passive protocols in real lost-dog scenarios.

## Open questions

- MacLean et al. 2019 (PNAS) is inaccessible but represents the strongest pro-breed-prior academic argument; a full read of that paper might tighten the h² estimates for stranger-fear specifically within sighthound-type breeds.
- Normando 2024 does not report the prevalence rate numerically beyond ">1 in 5" — the exact percentage and confidence interval for galgo stranger-fear prevalence would sharpen the probability prior. Accessing the full-text PDF (paywalled at EUR 10) would provide this.
- No study breaks down sighthound within-type differences by the specific galgo vs. greyhound vs. lurcher distinction. The Normando 2025 sighthound paper includes greyhound, saluki, whippet, etc., but may not include galgo español or podenco specifically — the overlap between this dataset and the Algarve population context is unclear.
- The galgo's specific history in Spain (hunting kennel conditions, minimal human socialization, systematic abandonment after hunting season — the "galguero" practice) may explain its elevated stranger-fear as environmental/developmental rather than genetic. If this is the case, a socialized galgo raised in a home would have a different prior than a hunting-kenneled galgo — intake context should modulate the breed prior.
