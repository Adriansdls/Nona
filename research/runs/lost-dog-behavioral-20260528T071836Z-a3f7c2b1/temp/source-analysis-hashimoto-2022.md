# Source Analysis — An agent-based model reveals lost person behavior based on data from wilderness search and rescue

**Original source:** [[an-agent-based-model-reveals-lost-person-behavior-based-on-data-from-wilderness]]
**Source type:** paper
**Source word count:** 8,912
**Your judgment:** Mathematical foundation and empirical validation of a behavioral-strategy PMF framework for lost-subject spatial probability — the primary quantitative prior for adapting reorientation-strategy distributions to lost dog categories.

*Suggested by [[an-agent-based-model-reveals-lost-person-behavior-based-on-data-from-wilderness]] — source analyst's digest of the full source body*

## Thesis / Central claim

Hashimoto et al. (2022) argue that lost person location probability can be modeled as a time-evolving spatial distribution generated by agents that draw reorientation strategies from a six-element probability mass function (PMF) at each time step, with independent realizations at every step. By exhaustively simulating all 462 possible strategy-weight combinations across 65 real ISRID hiker incidents and minimizing energy distance to actual find locations, they recover a quantitatively validated average behavioral profile for hikers: p_hiker = [RW=0.055, RT=0.377, DT=0.559, SP=0.003, VE=0.006, BT=0]. This is the first time-discrete agent-based lost-person model validated against real incident data rather than controlled experiments or summarized statistics.

## Methodology / Basis of claims

**Data:** N=65 hiker incidents from ISRID (augmented version), filtered to exclude find locations within 1 km of IPP, outside map limits, in inaccessible areas, or within 100 grid cells of the boundary. Full ISRID contains >145,000 incidents from around the world.

**Simulation architecture:**
- 20 km × 20 km maps per incident, discretized into 3000×3000 cells (6.67 m/cell), IPP at center (1500,1500)
- GIS layers from USGS: elevation (Gaussian gradient → Canny edge detection for ridge/drainage linear features), trails, roads, railroads, powerline easements, lake/river shorelines, inaccessible water interiors
- Time steps: K=100 hours simulated, T=850 steps/hour (based on max walking speed ~1.575 m/s)
- Smoothing factor α=0.55 for trajectory continuity: x(t+1) = (2-α)x(t) + (α-1)x(t-1) + α·v(t)
- 462 LPT PMFs tested (all distributions over 6 behaviors, incremented by 1/6, summing to 1)
- 500 Monte Carlo replicates per LPT per incident = 462 × 65 × 500 = ~15 million agent runs

**Fitting metric:** Energy statistic E(X,Y) = 2A - B - C where A = mean cross-distance between simulated closest-points X and real find location Y, B = mean self-distance within X, C = mean self-distance within Y (zero since Y is a single GPS point). Lowest E picks best LPT per incident.

**Aggregation:** Weighted average across incidents: w_i = (d_i / E_i)^(1/2) — incidents where the best LPT fits well relative to distance get higher weight. Normalized weighted average of per-incident best PMFs gives p_hiker.

**Validation:** Leave-one-out cross-validation (LOOCV): 58.5% of held-out incidents have LOOCV energy statistics above the 95th percentile of the 462-LPT distribution; 98.5% above the 50th percentile.

## Key findings / Claims (with specific numbers where present)

1. **Average hiker behavioral profile:** p_hiker = [RW=0.055, RT=0.377, DT=0.559, SP=0.003, VE=0.006, BT=0]. Route Traveling (37.7%) and Direction Traveling (55.9%) dominate. Staying Put, View Enhancing, and Backtracking are near-zero for hikers.

2. **LOOCV generalization:** 58.5% of held-out incidents have a LOOCV energy statistic above the 95th percentile of untrained distributions; 98.5% above the 50th percentile. The profile generalizes well across diverse landscapes.

3. **Effective speed:** Linear fit t = 4.31d + 13.31 (R²=0.44) for incidents with d<4 km gives effective walking speed of 0.064 m/s — far below the biomechanical maximum of 1.575 m/s, reflecting convoluted trajectories. For d>4 km, time-to-closest-point saturates at ~35 hours, suggesting a maximum mobile duration.

4. **Six reorientation strategies (PMF elements):** Random Walking (RW), Route Traveling (RT), Direction Traveling (DT), Staying Put (SP), View Enhancing (VE), Backtracking (BT). Each strategy defines a local 3×3 move-probability grid anchored to the agent's current velocity direction. RT enforces trail following; DT enforces straight persistence; VE selects highest adjacent elevation; BT steps backward on trajectory.

5. **Time-discrete independence assumption:** At each time step, the strategy is drawn as an *independent* realization of the PMF. The model carries one step of memory only through the smoothing factor α and the velocity direction — it does not model internal state evolution (e.g., fatigue, panic escalation) except via the saturation observed empirically.

6. **Map generation:** Canny edge detection on USGS elevation gradients extracts ridgelines and drainages as linear features. Lakes and wide rivers are inaccessible. Sufficiently steep terrain flagged as future inaccessible extension (not yet implemented).

7. **Dataset scale:** 46,609 lost persons in US National Parks 2004–2014, $51.4M cost. ISRID has >145,000 incidents globally. The paper uses 65 filtered hiker incidents.

8. **Behavioral interpretation:** DT dominance (55.9%) is consistent with hikers who travel cross-country in one compass direction; RT (37.7%) reflects trail reliance. ~1/3 of lost hikers historically move to higher elevation — model shows VE=0.006, suggesting the behavior exists but is infrequent in the hiker aggregate.

9. **No sighting/update mechanism in this paper:** The model does not include Bayesian updating from observations or sightings. It generates a prior spatial probability distribution from IPP forward. Sighting incorporation is explicitly absent — the model produces p(location|time, IPP, LPT) but does not handle p(location|time, IPP, LPT, sighting_data).

- **Koester (ISRID database)** — International Search and Rescue Incident Database, >145,000 incidents. The empirical backbone of all PMF fitting. Not publicly available in raw form.

## Caveats, limitations, contradictions

1. **No sighting update mechanism.** The model generates a forward spatial prior from IPP but provides no framework for updating that distribution when a sighting (confirmed or unconfirmed) is received. This is the most significant gap relative to the research query.

2. **Static LP assumption was deliberately relaxed** — but the replacement (closest-point-in-trajectory as the find proxy) introduces a different assumption: the model can't distinguish between "LP is at this location at time T" and "LP passed through this location at some point during 0–100 hours."

3. **No real trajectories in ISRID data.** Fitting uses only IPP and find location (two points), not the path. Trajectory shape is inferred, not directly validated.

4. **Temporal resolution absent.** Incidents lack timestamps for how long the LP was mobile before being found, so the model runs for the full 100-hour window and uses the closest point — this inflates the effective search area.

5. **Effective speed is very low (0.064 m/s)** — 25× slower than max walking speed. Authors acknowledge this and flag that adding more trajectory memory (inertia) would improve it.

6. **Hiker category only.** The 462-LPT grid and validation are done only for hikers, the most data-rich category. Other LPTs (children, dementia patients, hunters) would need separate runs and have sparser ISRID data.

7. **No land-cover impedance.** Vegetation density, slope steepness, and vision restriction are not modeled. Canny edge detection handles ridges/drainages but not dense brush.

8. **Overrepresentation bias in ISRID.** Only incidents where a search was mounted are recorded; most people who get lost reorient themselves and never appear in ISRID. The behavioral profiles may skew toward more disoriented or immobile subjects.

9. **Independence assumption per time step.** The PMF is applied as i.i.d. draws; real lost subjects likely have state-dependent strategy switching (increasing panic → more RT; fatigue → SP). The model captures only an aggregate stationary distribution.

## Relevance to research_query

This paper is directly load-bearing for the Nona/Red Cão lost-dog research query, specifically for the Bayesian updating from sightings sub-question. It provides the mathematical architecture for the **prior** — the spatial probability distribution p(location|time, IPP, behavioral_profile) — but explicitly does not include a sighting-update mechanism. The six-strategy PMF [RW, RT, DT, SP, VE, BT] maps cleanly onto dog behavioral categories: a fearful/shy dog would likely weight SP and RW heavily; a bold/active dog would weight DT and RT; a scent-tracking dog might need a seventh strategy not in this taxonomy. The weighted-average fitting procedure (equation 4–5) is directly reusable to derive dog-category priors from any accumulated lost-dog incident dataset. The LOOCV validation framework tells us how many incidents are needed before a category profile generalizes (>50 incidents gave 58.5% above 95th percentile; for dog categories with sparser data, generalization will be weaker). The critical missing piece for the research query — how a confirmed or unconfirmed sighting updates this distribution — requires the Bayesian extension found in Lin & Goodrich (2010) or WiSAR literature, not in this paper. Hashimoto 2022 provides the forward model; the Bayesian update layer sits on top of it.

## Extracted quotes

> "The behavior random variable selects from a distribution of six known lost person reorientation strategies to simulate the agent's trajectory."

Core framing: behavior as a PMF over strategies, not a deterministic rule — this is the property that makes Bayesian updating tractable.

> "The model defines how the LP moves between cells on the spatially discrete map. At every time step, the agent can move from its position to any of the eight adjacent cells, or stay in its current cell, by using an algorithm that defines possible behavior strategies."

Establishes the grid discretization contract (3000×3000 cells, 6.67 m each, 8-connected adjacency).

> "Independent realizations of this distribution of behaviors are generated at each time step and the agent's position is updated by the randomly selected strategy."

Confirms the i.i.d. assumption per step — the Markov property holds at the strategy level, enabling recursive Bayesian updates on the position distribution.

> "The resulting distribution p_hiker corresponds to a behavioral profile of [RW, RT, DT, SP, VE, BT] = [0.055, 0.377, 0.559, 0.003, 0.006, 0]"

The empirically validated human hiker prior. Baseline for dog category prior design via domain transfer.

> "58.5% of the N IPPs have energy statistic values above the 95th percentile, and 98.5% above the 50th percentile."

Quantifies the generalization strength of the fitted profile: strong for majority of novel incidents, near-universal at the median.

> "The effective speed is very slow. This is not entirely surprising, due to the convoluted nature of the simulated agent's paths, and we hypothesize that the speed would increase by including more memory into each updated time step by way of inertia."

Flags the trajectory-smoothness limitation — relevant for Nona because dog movement has stronger directional persistence than lost humans (particularly sight hounds).

> "Currently, these predictions are based on heuristics employed by the incident commander, which may not be able to simultaneously take into account geophysical and transient landscape features, and demographic and activity information about the LP."

Motivates the modeling project and implicitly motivates Nona's approach: replacing ad-hoc coordinator judgment with a probabilistic model informed by behavioral category.
