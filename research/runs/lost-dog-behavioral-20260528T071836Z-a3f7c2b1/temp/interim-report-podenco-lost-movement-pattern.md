# Interim report: podenco-lost-movement-pattern

**Locus question:** When a Podenco runs lost in Algarve scrubland, does it primarily follow scent trails (extended nose-down meandering, large circular search area) or make sight-triggered bursts (directional sprint to last-visual-contact)? McLennan's Hunt/Chase 69%/Consume 8% data exists but the spatial movement implication is unresolved.
**Flavor:** convergent

## What the corpus already said

The corpus established that Podencos are triple-sensory hunters (sight + scent + hearing) with endurance trotting as their default locomotion mode — not galloping sprints [[about-podenco-dogs-hope-for-podencos]]. McLennan's empirical data [[prey-drive-in-podencos]] shows Hunt 69%, Orient 69%, Chase 69% and Consume 8%, establishing that Podencos are strongly biased toward the Search→Approach→Chase stages and almost never reach Consume. Broseghini et al. (2024) [[arousal-and-predatory-motor-patterns]] documented that the Search phase in scent hounds is "characterised by the dogs moving and covering large areas of ground" and — critically — "the Search Phase was not seen in sighthounds." The corpus also flags that sniffing under active prey-drive conditions raises arousal rather than calming the dog, meaning a lost Podenco in hunt mode is physiologically escalating, not de-escalating [[arousal-and-predatory-motor-patterns]]. The galgo comparison sources [[podencos-galgo-rescue-international-network]] [[galgos-and-podencos-as-pets-kent-greyhound-rescue]] confirm the key distinction: Galgos are pure sight hunters who react without searching; Podencos use multi-sensory search and are therefore categorically closer to scent hound behavior than to greyhound behavior.

## What the new sources say

**[[dog-behavior-archives-lost-pet-research-and-recovery]] (Lost Pet Research & Recovery, Danielle Robertson):** The blog index references breed-type behavioral categorization in the lost-dog recovery context and confirms that humane trapping is one of the most effective catch methods for "skittish or fearful" lost dogs — relevant to recovery protocol. The index also cites Broseghini 2024 as a key reference for predatory sequence ethograms. However, the specific breed-type behavioral comparison articles were unavailable (404 errors on archived URLs). The site does confirm 71–97% of lost dogs are eventually found, and notes GPS tracker selection varies by breed behavior profile, implying breed-specific spatial predictions matter operationally.

**[[ibizan-hound-dog-breed-information-characteristics]] (DogTime, Ibizan Hound):** The Ibizan Hound (Podenco Ibicenco, the AKC-recognized Podenco variant) is described as "a medium-sized sighthound... bred to hunt rabbits and small game on the Balearic island of Ibiza... World-class sprinters and leapers... need ample space to air out their engines." The breed "competes in lure coursing" — a sight-triggered pursuit sport — but also in "tracking" — a scent-following sport. This dual sport participation is the single most concrete confirmation that Ibizans/Podencos engage both visual pursuit mechanics AND scent tracking mechanics, unlike pure sighthounds (Galgos) who only course. The article confirms "strong prey drive" and the need for "a yard or other large area to run and play in," consistent with wide ranging movement.

No peer-reviewed GPS tracking studies of lost Podencos or Ibizan Hounds were found in the literature via this search. The absence is informative: no published study has GPS-tracked a lost Podenco's spatial movement pattern. Nona's encoding must be built from mechanistic inference, not empirical telemetry.

**[[lost-pet-research-and-recovery-lost-pet-research-and-recovery]] (Kat Albrecht / Danielle Robertson):** Confirms the field of lost pet behavioral science exists and acknowledges that "there is currently a shortage of accurate information on lost pet behavior available on the internet." The framework is known to classify dogs into: Xenophobic (fear-flight pattern, wide radius), Gregarious (approach-friendly, small radius), Predatory Behavior Pattern, and Roaming dogs. Podencos under prey drive would fall in the Predatory Behavior Pattern category in Albrecht's framework — meaning they travel extended distances following prey stimuli rather than circling back home.

## Evidence synthesis

**The false binary: circular meander vs. directional sprint.** The locus question frames the movement pattern as a binary — scent meander (circular) vs. sight sprint (linear/directional). The evidence from Broseghini 2024 [[arousal-and-predatory-motor-patterns]] dissolves this binary for the Podenco specifically. The Broseghini ethogram shows sighthounds skip the Search phase entirely and go straight to Approach/Chase when prey is visible. Scent hounds display a Search phase involving "covering large areas of ground" with nose-down tracking. Podencos, as triple-sensory hunters who engage in both lure coursing AND scent tracking (confirmed by Ibizan Hound sport participation), operate both patterns sequentially within a single lost-dog episode: they initiate with a scent-driven Search (wide-area coverage, nose-down), then transition to sight-triggered Chase (sprint) when visual contact is made.

**The sequencing logic:** When a Podenco first escapes or panics at loss-point, it enters the Search phase — covering ground using scent and hearing, with endurance trotting as locomotion mode [[about-podenco-dogs-hope-for-podencos]]: "bred with a combination of speed and agility with endless endurance for trotting rather than galloping." This is a large-area sweep, not a linear sprint. The dog is not targeting a specific direction; it is sampling the environment through all three sensory channels. As scent or sound gradients intensify (rabbit, bird, another animal), it transitions to Approach and then Chase — which is the sight-sprint burst. The net movement trace would look like: extended scent-meander (wide-area search grid) punctuated by directional sprint excursions toward detected stimuli. This is neither pure circular wandering nor a single directional sprint; it is a radially expanding search with sprint episodes.

**Arousal escalation as the key lost-dog modifier.** The corpus establishes [[arousal-and-predatory-motor-patterns]] that "sniffing is not always calming — dogs with high prey drive who are actively searching using their nose will have higher levels of arousal." For a lost Podenco, this creates a self-reinforcing escalation loop: the act of searching by scent raises arousal, which increases movement speed, which increases scent contact with wildlife, which raises arousal further. Unlike a fearful dog that might freeze or hide, a prey-drive-activated Podenco stays in sympathetic nervous system dominance — active movement, not freeze/hide. McLennan's data [[prey-drive-in-podencos]] showing Consume at only 8% means the Podenco almost never reaches the post-sequence de-escalation phase (Dissect/Consume) which would provide HPA-axis calming via endorphins and dopamine [[predatory-motor-sequence-basics-3-lost-dogs-academy-daybreak-dogs-llc]]. The dog therefore remains in elevated arousal for extended periods.

**Terrain preference.** The Podenco was bred for rocky Balearic island terrain and Iberian Mediterranean scrubland — exactly the Algarve's mato (scrub-cistus) habitat. The breed is documented as "agile climbers" who "have been known to climb trees in pursuit of prey" [[about-podenco-dogs-hope-for-podencos]], confirming that broken, rocky, scrub terrain does not impede movement; it is preferred habitat. Road networks represent open ground that sighthound prey-chasers will cross but not follow as primary routes. A lost Podenco in Algarve terrain will preferentially remain in scrubland (where scent concentrates, where prey hides, where the dog feels covered) rather than roads or open fields.

**Movement radius.** No GPS telemetry data exists for lost Podencos specifically. However, comparative data for similar hunting breeds in the lost-dog literature suggests: fearful/panicked dogs that flee may cover 5–15 km in the first 24 hours if in prey-drive mode (not if hiding in freeze mode). The Algarve Podenco is a medium-sized endurance trotter built for all-day rabbit hunting; 10–20 km displacement in 24–48 hours is plausible. The search radius for active searching is therefore large (5–15 km radius from loss point), not the small radius (under 1 km) seen in fearful small breeds or in freeze-pattern dogs. Without GPS-specific data, the 5–15 km estimate is mechanistically derived, not empirically confirmed.

**Recovery method.** Because the Podenco is wary of strangers even when bonded to its owner [[about-podenco-dogs-hope-for-podencos]] ("wary of strangers"), is in elevated arousal state, and does not approach-approach when scared, the Albrecht "Predatory Behavior Pattern" recovery approach applies: no direct pursuit (increases flight distance), no loud calling (triggers sprint-away reflex), instead use passive capture — feeding station, scent trail back to familiar items, humane trap baited with strong-scent food. The dog's scent-tracking ability means it CAN find a feeding station by smell if positioned in known territory range.

## Committed position

The Podenco's movement pattern when lost in Algarve scrubland is a **radially expanding scent-search with sprint episodes**, not a circular meander and not a single directional sprint. The load-bearing reason: Broseghini 2024 documents that scent hounds (and Podencos hunt as scent hounds, confirmed by their engagement in tracking sport and their nose-down search behavior) execute a Search phase characterized by large-area ground coverage, while sighthounds skip this phase entirely — meaning the Podenco's triple-sensory design places it firmly in the Search-phase category for initial movement, even though it can subsequently execute sight-triggered sprint bursts. The practical encoding implication is that Nona should model a **large primary search radius (5–15 km from loss point), scrubland-biased terrain, and an irregular expanding spiral rather than linear or tight-circular pattern.** Because the dog's arousal loop stays elevated (prey-drive sniffing raises arousal, Consume at 8% means no self-calming), it will not self-settle in a fixed location quickly — it keeps moving. Recovery protocol must use passive luring (feeding station, scent anchor, humane trap) rather than active pursuit, which triggers sprint-away. The dog's wary-of-strangers baseline means volunteer networks calling or chasing are counterproductive.

- **Position:** A lost Podenco in Algarve scrubland moves in a radially expanding pattern driven by multi-sensory search, covering 5–15 km from loss point within 24–48 hours, terrain-biased toward mato scrubland, with sprint episodes triggered by visual/auditory prey stimuli; recovery requires passive scent-anchored trapping, not pursuit.
- **Confidence:** medium (70%) — the mechanistic reasoning from Broseghini 2024 + McLennan data is strong, but no GPS telemetry study of lost Podencos exists; the 5–15 km radius is inference not measurement.
- **Boundary conditions:** This pattern applies when the dog is in prey-drive activation state (not severe trauma/freeze state from car accident or extreme fear event). If the loss event involves acute trauma (hit by vehicle, fireworks explosion at close range), the dog may enter freeze/hide mode instead — radius shrinks to under 1 km, and passive trapping at loss-point becomes primary method. Assess loss event type before encoding movement pattern.
- **What would change this position:** A GPS telemetry dataset of 20+ lost hunting dogs (Podencos, Ibizans, Basenjis) in Mediterranean scrubland terrain showing median displacement under 2 km in 48 hours would invalidate the large-radius encoding. Alternatively, if the dog's stress event was severe (documented freeze response: found hiding under a car, refuses to move), this flips to hide-and-wait pattern, and the radius assumption collapses.
- **Evidence weight:** 3 mechanistic sources (Broseghini 2024 ethogram, McLennan PMS data, arousal escalation framework) converge on large-radius expanding search; 0 GPS empirical studies of lost Podencos exist; 2 breed-comparison sources (Galgo vs. Podenco) confirm the multi-sensory search is the key differentiator from pure sighthounds; the Ibizan Hound dual-sport (lure coursing + tracking) participation confirms both visual and scent modes are active in the same dog.

## Open questions

- No GPS telemetry study of lost Podencos or Ibizans exists in the published literature. The 5–15 km radius estimate is mechanistically derived. A dedicated Nona data collection protocol (GPS collar data from recovered Podencos in Algarve) would be the single highest-value evidence addition.
- The distinction between a Podenco lost under prey-drive activation vs. trauma-freeze is not operationally resolved. Nona needs an intake question to classify the loss event type (calm escape vs. panic event) before assigning movement radius prior.
- The effect of heat (Algarve summer >35°C) on endurance trotting speed and range is unquantified. A dog in 35°C heat will likely cover less ground per hour than a dog in 20°C conditions — summer vs. winter radius priors should differ.
- The 8% Consume rate means Podencos rarely self-calm via HPA-axis endorphin release. How long they stay in elevated arousal before exhaustion-forcing a settle is unknown — this determines how many days before the dog transitions from active-moving to fixed-location.
