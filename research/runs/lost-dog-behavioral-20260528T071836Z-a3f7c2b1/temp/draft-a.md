# Probabilistic Behavioral Engine for the Nona / Red Cão Algarve Lost-Dog Platform — Breadth Draft

## Executive Summary

The behavioral engine for Nona should be built as a four-axis probabilistic system whose inputs are time-elapsed, breed/temperament, escape-trigger type, and sighting history, and whose outputs are (a) a spatial probability map updated by sightings and (b) a temperament-and-phase-gated action selector that determines whether active search, passive monitoring, or full lockdown is appropriate. The evidence base supports this structure with high confidence even though the underlying empirical literature on lost dogs is thin. The most robust single quantitative anchor is Kremer's Dallas analysis of 30,609 shelter intakes: 70% of returned-to-owner strays were found within 1 mile of home and 42% within 400 feet [[frontiers-a-new-web-based-tool-for-rto-focused-animal-shelter-data-analysis]]. The most robust temporal anchor is Lord 2007b's median recovery time of 2 days with a 0.5–21 day range across 187 Ohio dogs [[lost-pet-statistics-lost-pet-research-and-recovery]], reinforced by Weiss 2012's 93% (86–97% CI) all-cause recovery rate across 1,015 US households [[frequency-of-lost-dogs-and-cats-in-the-united-states-and-the-methods-used-to-loc]]. The most robust mechanistic anchor is the convergent practitioner consensus across Kat Albrecht's Missing Animal Response (MAR) framework, Lost Dogs Illinois's 13-year case database, and Lost Dogs of America's thousands-of-cases mortality data: search pressure (calling, chasing, crowd convergence) is the dominant cause of recovery failure and death — top three lost-dog death causes are vehicle strike, train strike, and drowning, all attributable to flight under search pressure [[never-follow-chase-or-pressure-a-lost-dog-lost-dogs-of-america]].

For the Algarve specifically, the engine must hard-code two breed-specific priors: a galgo (Spanish greyhound) prior that combines a dual chase/avoidance system (greyhound prey-fixation OR=8.34 for spatial search anchored at prey-loss terminus [[effects-of-lure-type-on-chase-related-behaviour-in-racing-greyhounds-pmc]]; galgo-specific stranger-fear at p=0.009 for human-approach protocol [[prevalence-of-some-behavioural-problems-in-two-groups-of-rescue-dogs-galgos-from]]); and a podenco prior built around multi-sensory hunting with a 69% Hunt/Chase rate and an 8% Consume rate that makes food lures fail during active arousal [[prey-drive-in-podencos]] [[arousal-and-predatory-motor-patterns]]. The phase model that applies to the median dog (Phase 1 = 0–72h cooperative; Phase 2 = 72h–7d survival mode; Phase 3 = 7d+ entrenched) [[interim-report-dog-phase-transition-thresholds]] does not generalize cleanly to galgo or to podenco prey-drive escapes; for those subpopulations, Phase 1 collapses toward zero and the engine must treat day-zero behavior as already Phase 2. Sighting reliability should be encoded as a graded λ-weight (camera ≈0.95, daylight eyewitness ≈0.70, night eyewitness ≈0.35, crowd-contaminated ≈0.20), and the engine must enforce a strict action gate that separates belief update (always update the map) from response action (never deploy a crowd for a fearful dog regardless of sighting quality) [[interim-report-bayesian-sighting-update]].

The remainder of this document treats each of the four atomic topics — temporal phases, breed-specific priors, Bayesian updating, and fear escalation — with the four required outputs (empirical findings, calibration numbers, knowledge gaps, practical encoding) at the breadth required to cover every named entity in the decomposition.

## 1. Temporal Behavioral Phases — Empirical Data

### Key Empirical Findings

The lost-dog literature does not contain a peer-reviewed study that directly measures behavioral phase transitions in dogs. The phase model used operationally by Missing Animal Response, Lost Dogs of America, Lost Dogs Illinois, K9s On Call, and Buddha Dog Rescue is a practitioner consensus assembled from thousands of case observations, not an experimental result [[interim-report-dog-phase-transition-thresholds]] [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]]. The strongest direct quantitative dog evidence is Lord 2007b's median recovery time of 2 days, with a 0.5–21 day range across n=187 Ohio dogs [[lost-pet-statistics-lost-pet-research-and-recovery]], and Weiss 2012's nationally representative recovery rate of 93% across n=1,015 US households [[frequency-of-lost-dogs-and-cats-in-the-united-states-and-the-methods-used-to-loc]]. Huang 2018's cat curve provides the only structurally comparable temporal recovery distribution: 34% of missing cats found alive by day 7, 50% by day 30, 56% by day 61, then near-plateau [[source-analysis-huang-2018-missing-cats-search-methods-locations]]. The cat curve transfers in shape, not in absolute values — dogs recover faster than cats because they self-present to humans more readily and shelter reclaim rates are an order of magnitude higher (26–40% for dogs vs. 2–4% for cats).

Kremer 2021 provides the dominant secondary empirical anchor: 91% of shelter-RTO dogs were reclaimed within Dallas's 5-day mandatory hold period, with the post-hold length-of-stay median collapsing to 0 days [[frontiers-a-new-web-based-tool-for-rto-focused-animal-shelter-data-analysis]] [[source-analysis-kremer-2021-rto-spatial-analysis-dallas-n30609]]. This is not a behavioral measurement per se, but it establishes day 5 as a sharp operational urgency cliff. Lost Dogs Illinois states from 3,000+ recoveries over 13 years that "after 3-4 days, if your dog has been separated from you and is in flight/survivor mode, from their perspective they don't have an owner anymore. Anyone who approaches them is a stranger, including you" [[lost-dog-behavior-tips-lost-dogs-illinois]]. Buddha Dog Rescue frames survival-mode probability as "50-75-90-99% depending on dog's history" and notes that, once in survival mode, "very often EVERY human is viewed as a predator – even the one that has fed, loved, walked, bathed and pampered them for the last 2-4-12 years" [[survival-mode-buddha-dog-rescue-recovery]]. The hormonal substrate is well-documented: Hennessy's SPARCS 2015 shelter-dog cortisol data shows a three-phase HPA arc — peak elevation days 1–3 (≈3× home baseline), declining days 4–9, and physiological adaptation/plateau from day 9 onward — which provides mechanistic plausibility for the practitioner-named day-3 to day-4 behavioral cliff [[interim-report-dog-phase-transition-thresholds]].

The behavioral signatures of each phase converge across sources. K9s On Call describes Phase-2 dogs as "more likely to be moving at night" and "when they stop moving, they will usually hunker down for the day" [[understanding-survival-mode-behavior-in-lost-dogs-k9s-on-call-missing-pet-servic]]. The same source emphasizes the "Silence Factor": survival-mode dogs typically do not bark, making auditory search useless once a dog has shifted phase. Buddha Dog adds the food-container paradox: shaking food containers, normally a positive stimulus, becomes a flight trigger in survival mode [[survival-mode-buddha-dog-rescue-recovery]]. Kat Albrecht's MAR taxonomy is the dominant practitioner schema and recognizes that escape circumstance modulates phase entry speed: opportunistic escapes (gate left open) produce slow meandering; wanderlust escapes (active gate-jumping) produce moderate dispersal; blind panic escapes (fireworks, traumatic event, xenophobic temperament) produce immediate Phase-2 behavior with rapid long-distance displacement [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]] [[what-you-dont-know-about-lost-pets-can-hurt-them]].

Recovery-rate distribution by phase is partially reconstructible. Lord 2007b reports 8% of dogs returned home unassisted, 71% found within 1 mile, 14% found 1–5 miles, and 7% found more than 5 miles from home; combined with the 2-day median recovery, this means the bulk of recoveries occur during Phase 1 [[lost-pet-statistics-lost-pet-research-and-recovery]]. Weiss 2012's recovery method breakdown — 49% neighborhood search, 20% spontaneous return, 15% ID tag/chip, 6% shelter — is also concentrated in early phases [[frequency-of-lost-dogs-and-cats-in-the-united-states-and-the-methods-used-to-loc]]. HASS GPS-tracker data from Fi collars synthesized by Foster 2025 finds 95% of dogs that wander beyond safe zones travel at most 1.8 miles (~2 km) from home, with average recovery distance just 700 feet [[most-lost-dogs-are-just-around-the-corner-literally-hass]]. These are dominated by gregarious dogs found before they could enter Phase 2.

The interpretive beat that the sources do not state but the convergence forces: lost-dog recovery is a race against phase transition. The shape of recovery follows two superimposed curves — the gregarious sub-population resolves quickly (most Lord and Weiss recoveries), and the xenophobic / blind-panic sub-population grinds through a much longer tail in which recovery becomes contingent on disciplined passive protocol. The 2-day median is not a description of a single population; it is the centroid of a bimodal distribution. Nona's engine must not treat the population mean as the prior for any individual dog.

### Calibration Numbers

- **Phase 1 (acute / cooperative)**: 0–72 hours from escape. Direct approach by owner viable for the median dog. Active neighborhood search beneficial. Empirical anchor: Lord 2007b median 2 days [[lost-pet-statistics-lost-pet-research-and-recovery]]; physiologic anchor: peak HPA activation days 1–3 [[interim-report-dog-phase-transition-thresholds]]. Confidence: medium for the universal 72h boundary; high that the median dog is recoverable in this window.
- **Phase 2 (survival / passive)**: 72 hours through 7 days. Most dogs perceive humans as threats, including owner. Trap deployment becomes mandatory. Empirical anchor: 5-day shelter hold cliff (Kremer 2021, 91% within hold) [[frontiers-a-new-web-based-tool-for-rto-focused-animal-shelter-data-analysis]]; behavioral anchor: Lost Dogs Illinois 3–4 day threshold [[lost-dog-behavior-tips-lost-dogs-illinois]].
- **Phase 3 (entrenched survival)**: 7 days and beyond. Recovery requires sighting-driven trap-and-camera methodology only; success rates collapse. Cat-curve structural anchor: Huang 2018 found-alive flatlines after day 61 [[source-analysis-huang-2018-missing-cats-search-methods-locations]]. Dogs with strong xenophobic baseline may stabilize in a territory and survive months (Winnie's 69-day recovery) [[dog-befriends-a-fox-while-lost-in-blizzard]].
- **Recovery-by-phase distribution (operational estimate)**: ~60–70% of all recoveries occur in Phase 1; ~20–25% in Phase 2; ~5–15% in Phase 3. Derived from Lord 2007b 2-day median plus Huang day-7 (34%) / day-30 (50%) curve adjusted for the dog/cat 13× shelter reclaim ratio [[source-analysis-huang-2018-missing-cats-search-methods-locations]] [[lost-pet-statistics-lost-pet-research-and-recovery]].
- **Cortisol HPA arc (mechanistic)**: days 1–3 peak elevation (~3× home baseline), days 4–9 declining, day 9+ adaptation plateau [[interim-report-dog-phase-transition-thresholds]].
- **Survival-mode onset probability range**: 50–99% depending on dog history, with xenophobic / trauma-history dogs near 99% and gregarious / no-trauma dogs near 50% [[survival-mode-buddha-dog-rescue-recovery]].
- **Phase-1 collapse modifiers**: For galgos, effective Phase 1 ≈ 0 hours (stranger-approach success rate day 1 = 0.02 per practitioner consensus) [[interim-report-breed-prior-individual-vs-population]]. For podenco prey-drive escapes, effective Phase 1 ≈ 0–4 hours before high-arousal active ranging [[interim-report-podenco-lost-movement-pattern]].
- **Algarve rural modifier**: In low-density rural terrain typical of Algarve mato, expect Phase 2 to extend up to 10+ days because the dog encounters no pursuing humans to push it into deeper Phase 3 [[interim-report-dog-phase-transition-thresholds]].

### Knowledge Gaps

- No peer-reviewed dog study has measured behavioral state at discrete time points (24h, 48h, 72h, 96h, 7d) under field-loss conditions. The 72h Phase 1/2 boundary and the 7d Phase 2/3 boundary are practitioner anchors, not measurements. The IAABC Foundation explicitly notes that "no one had ever studied lost pet behaviors" until Kat Albrecht began informal collection [[interim-report-dog-phase-transition-thresholds]].
- Hennessy's cortisol arc was measured in shelter-confined dogs, not free-ranging lost dogs. Translation from confinement stress to displacement stress is mechanistically plausible but unmeasured [[interim-report-dog-phase-transition-thresholds]].
- The Lord 2007b 2-day median is from a single Ohio county; the dataset is survivorship-biased (recovered dogs only). The right tail (dogs that took weeks or never recovered) is underrepresented [[lost-pet-statistics-lost-pet-research-and-recovery]].
- Reversibility of Phase 2 — whether a dog can be coaxed back into cooperative behavior via a stable feeding station without the full transition resetting — is undocumented quantitatively. Practitioner anecdote (Winnie, 69 days) suggests yes [[dog-befriends-a-fox-while-lost-in-blizzard]].
- Onset time is genuinely variable within a temperament category. Buddha Dog and Holiday Barn both flag that a given dog's transition can be instantaneous or take a week, "with very little correlation with previous behavior/life history" [[survival-mode-buddha-dog-rescue-recovery]]. This variance is encoded but cannot be reduced.
- Heat/season modulation in Mediterranean climates: an Algarve summer (35°C+) compresses Phase 2 daytime movement to early morning and evening; this shifts the behavioral signature without changing the underlying phase. No published data quantifies this.

### Practical Encoding

Encode the phase model as a probability distribution over phase membership conditioned on (time_elapsed, temperament_class, escape_trigger). The phase membership is not deterministic. For a default dog at time T:

```
P(Phase 1 | T) = sigmoid_decay(T, midpoint=48h, slope=24h)
P(Phase 2 | T) = bump(T, peak=120h, sigma=72h)
P(Phase 3 | T) = sigmoid_grow(T, midpoint=168h, slope=48h)
```

The midpoint of the Phase 1 decay shifts to T≈4h for podenco prey-drive escape and to T≈0 for xenophobic-classified galgo. The midpoint shifts to T≈120h for low-density Algarve mato terrain with no pursuing humans. Each user-visible recommendation should display the dominant phase and the probability of being in that phase, never a hard cutoff. The action selector reads phase distribution × temperament class × current sighting state and outputs one of {active_search_recommended, passive_only, full_lockdown}. Active search is permitted only when P(Phase 1) > 0.6 and temperament_class != xenophobic and no prior failed approach attempts have been recorded. Trap-deployment authorization fires when P(Phase 2 or Phase 3) > 0.5 and camera-confirmed feeding pattern exists at a specific station for ≥48 hours. The 5-day shelter cliff should drive an urgency multiplier on alert prioritization, decaying after day 5 with a half-life of 7 days; cases at day 30+ are deprioritized but never closed because of the long-tail Huang/Winnie pattern.

## 2. Breed-Specific Behavioral Priors

### Key Empirical Findings

The single most important empirical fact about breed is that it is a weak predictor of individual behavior. Morrill et al. (2022, Science, n=18,385 surveyed, n=2,155 genome-sequenced) demonstrate that breed explains just 9% of behavioral variation in individual dogs, and agonistic threshold — the factor most directly relevant to flight/evasion in lost-dog scenarios — is the *least* breed-predictable behavioral dimension; the authors state explicitly that "breed is almost uninformative" for that factor [[source-analysis-morrill-et-al-2022-ancestry-inclusive-dog-genomics]] [[ancestry-inclusive-dog-genomics-challenges-popular-breed-stereotypes-pmc]]. Behavioral GWAS loci in dogs are not unusually differentiated across breeds (PBS z = −0.001, p = 0.224), whereas aesthetic-trait loci are; this means modern breed formation (~160 years) did not selectively fix behavioral variants [[source-analysis-morrill-et-al-2022-ancestry-inclusive-dog-genomics]]. Within every breed of 25+ dogs in the Darwin's Ark sample, 67.2 ± 7.5% scored within one standard deviation of the population mean on any behavioral factor [[source-analysis-morrill-et-al-2022-ancestry-inclusive-dog-genomics]]. Sixty-six percent of mixed-breed dogs carry meaningful ancestry (>5%) from four or more breeds; only 17% are two-breed mixes [[source-analysis-morrill-et-al-2022-ancestry-inclusive-dog-genomics]]. Operationally, this means visual breed identification of mixed-breed dogs cannot reliably predict their behavioral profile.

This must be held in tension with a second equally robust empirical fact: group-level breed means differ significantly for specific traits, and these group means matter operationally. Normando et al. (2024, n=410 rescue dogs, 198 galgos + 212 controls) found that galgos showed "out of context fear of non-cohabiting adult people" at significantly higher prevalence than other rescue dogs (p=0.009, >1 in 5 galgos vs. effectively zero in controls) [[prevalence-of-some-behavioural-problems-in-two-groups-of-rescue-dogs-galgos-from]]. Normando et al. (2025, n=768, C-BARQ) confirms within-sighthound behavioral differences: predatory drive differs significantly across sighthound breeds (p<0.001), with Saluki / Cirneco highest and Piccolo Levriero Italiano lowest [[behavioural-characteristics-of-sighthounds-an-exploratory-investigation-dog-beha]]. Sighthounds as a group display "very low level of aggressive behaviour... combined with high predatory drive and a fair level of attachment/affection" [[behavioural-characteristics-of-sighthounds-an-exploratory-investigation-dog-beha]]. The resolution is scope-based: breed is uninformative for *individual* prediction but real and operational for *strategy defaults at the population level* [[interim-report-breed-prior-individual-vs-population]].

**Sighthounds (galgo, greyhound).** Starling et al. (2020) measured prey-fixation behavior in 626 racing greyhounds and found an odds ratio of 8.34 (95% CI 1.72–42.38, p=0.009) for fixation at the lure's last-known-location when the lure stopped and went silent versus when it remained audible and visible [[effects-of-lure-type-on-chase-related-behaviour-in-racing-greyhounds-pmc]] [[source-analysis-effects-of-lure-type-on-chase-behaviour-in-greyhounds]]. Chase is appetitive and self-reinforcing — wild canid hunt success rates of 5–13% and greyhound coursing capture rates of ~13% demonstrate that the behavior is maintained without consumption. Sound is the dominant chase-initiation cue per practitioner consensus, more salient than visual stimuli [[source-analysis-effects-of-lure-type-on-chase-behaviour-in-greyhounds]]. Galgos and English greyhounds share centuries of sighthound selective breeding; the predatory motor sequence (skip Search, go direct to Orient→Chase) is phylogenetically conserved [[interim-report-galgo-vs-greyhound-behavioral-equivalence]] [[arousal-and-predatory-motor-patterns]]. Galgos can clear 6-foot fences from a standstill; SOS Galgos and Galgos del Sol report 18+ individual cases of hide-in-corner / flight-from-approach behavior at rescue intake, and Galgos del Sol observes that abandoned galgos may need "weeks—or sometimes months" of patient observation before allowing human approach [[sos-galgos-adopt-a-survivor]] [[what-is-a-galgo-galgos-del-sol]] [[galgo-espaol-animal-corner]]. English racing greyhounds, by contrast, exhibit a freeze response far more often than flight when stressed — the 4F response sequence (Fidget/Freeze/Flight/Fight) is documented in the Greyhounds as Pets NZ guide [[2-fear-and-anxiety-greyhounds-as-pets]]. The galgo and greyhound therefore share a chase prior but diverge sharply on the approach-avoidance prior.

**Scent hounds (podenco, beagle).** Podencos are explicitly triple-sensory hunters — sight, scent, and hearing — bred for rabbit hunting across Spain, with endurance-trotting locomotion rather than greyhound-style sprint and "endless endurance" [[about-podenco-dogs-hope-for-podencos]] [[podencos-galgo-rescue-international-network]]. McLennan's published predatory-motor-sequence data for podencos yields percentages of Hunt 69%, Orient 69%, Eye 54%, Stalk 23%, Chase 69%, Grab-bite 69%, Possess 31%, Kill-bite 46%, Dissect 23%, Consume 8% [[prey-drive-in-podencos]]. The 8% Consume figure is the load-bearing operational insight: podencos hunt intrinsically, not for food reward, making food lures unreliable during active arousal [[arousal-and-predatory-motor-patterns]]. Podencos can climb trees in pursuit of prey, are agile across Mediterranean scrubland, and can clear 6-foot fences [[about-podenco-dogs-hope-for-podencos]]. Beagles, the dominant scent-hound reference outside Iberia, possess ~220 million olfactory receptors and can detect a scent trail from 1 mile away; un-neutered males detect a female in heat from 2–3 miles [[beagle-running-away-chasing-escaping]]. When a beagle escapes, the canonical reading is that it runs *toward* a scent or another dog, not *away from* the owner; high-density scent treats (bacon, chicken juices) can redirect a running beagle only at short range [[beagle-running-away-chasing-escaping]]. The scent-hound profile is fundamentally different from the sighthound profile: scent hounds execute the Search phase visibly (nose-down, large-area ground coverage), while sighthounds skip it [[arousal-and-predatory-motor-patterns]].

**Toy breeds.** Albrecht's 1999 informal survey (n=254) — the only data anchor for toy-breed lost-dog distance — found that toy breeds rarely exceeded three-quarter mile from origin [[interim-report-breed-prior-individual-vs-population]]. Pet FBI's practitioner synthesis aligns: small dogs typically stay within a 1–2 mile radius [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]]. Mortality risk is elevated for toy breeds because their primary predators (coyote in North America, fox / hunting dog encounter in Algarve) treat them as prey rather than as conspecifics.

**Herding breeds.** Sources do not provide quantitative herding-breed lost-dog data. Lost Dogs Illinois flags herding breeds as elevated-flight-risk in their 3,000+ case review, noting that "puppy mill dogs, rescue dogs, and breeds with strong herding or guarding instincts" are predisposed to immediate Phase 2 entry [[lost-dog-behavior-tips-lost-dogs-illinois]]. Border collie ancestry has a confirmed genetic effect on biddability (LMER t=−4.6, pFDR=0.0002), but biddability is not directly mapped to lost-dog behavior [[source-analysis-morrill-et-al-2022-ancestry-inclusive-dog-genomics]].

**Guardian breeds.** No empirical study isolates guardian-breed lost-dog behavior in the corpus. The practitioner heuristic from MAR is that aloof / guardian temperaments behave like xenophobic temperaments in displacement: avoiding human contact, traveling moderate-to-long distances, and requiring passive recovery [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]]. The same Albrecht categorization places guardian breeds with significant territorial baseline at higher risk of returning to original territory if loss occurs near home, but at higher risk of permanent displacement if loss occurs in unfamiliar territory.

**Mixed / unknown breeds.** Albrecht's 1999 informal data showed mixed-breed dogs averaging 14 miles before pickup vs. 2 miles for purebreds — implying that the population of dogs that travel farthest is dominated by mixed breeds, but this is almost certainly a survivorship artifact of which dogs reach the population of dogs that "travel far before pickup" rather than a genetic effect [[interim-report-breed-prior-individual-vs-population]]. The Morrill 2022 finding that 66% of mixed-breed dogs carry ancestry from 4+ breeds means the engine cannot safely apply any single-breed-derived prior to a mixed dog [[source-analysis-morrill-et-al-2022-ancestry-inclusive-dog-genomics]]. Mixed-breed dogs should default to population-level priors, modulated only by individual behavioral signals.

The interpretive synthesis the sources will not state but the evidence forces: breed is a *capture-strategy prior* and not a *probability prior*. A galgo intake should set defaults to passive-only capture protocols not because galgos are individually predictable but because the asymmetric cost of mis-classifying a fearful galgo as gregarious (fatal crowd-displacement) vastly exceeds the cost of mis-classifying a gregarious galgo as fearful (a few wasted hours of trap setup) [[interim-report-breed-prior-individual-vs-population]].

- **Galgo (Spanish greyhound)**:

  - Stranger-fear group prevalence: **>20% of galgos** (Normando 2024, p=0.009) [[prevalence-of-some-behavioural-problems-in-two-groups-of-rescue-dogs-galgos-from]].

  - Greyhound-specific fear response: **freeze-dominant** (Fidget/Freeze/Flight/Fight) [[2-fear-and-anxiety-greyhounds-as-pets]].

  - Beagle olfactory receptors: **~220M**; scent detection range: **~1 mile** [[beagle-running-away-chasing-escaping]].
  - Movement: scent-following over long distances; can override well-trained recall [[beagle-running-away-chasing-escaping]].
  - Predatory motor sequence: **execute Search stage visibly** (nose-down, large-area sweep) [[arousal-and-predatory-motor-patterns]].
- **Toy breeds**: **<1.2 km (0.75 mile) movement radius** typical [[interim-report-breed-prior-individual-vs-population]] [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]].
- **Herding breeds**: classified by MAR practitioners as **elevated flight-risk / Phase-2-prone**; no quantitative radius anchor [[lost-dog-behavior-tips-lost-dogs-illinois]].
- **Guardian breeds**: practitioner classification as **aloof / Phase-2-prone**, behavior similar to xenophobic temperament class [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]].
- **Mixed / unknown breeds**: default to **population priors** (70% within 1 mile / 95% within 1.8 mi per Kremer 2021 + HASS Fi GPS) [[frontiers-a-new-web-based-tool-for-rto-focused-animal-shelter-data-analysis]] [[most-lost-dogs-are-just-around-the-corner-literally-hass]].

### Knowledge Gaps

- No peer-reviewed controlled study compares lost-dog recovery rate, distance, or method across breed types. Albrecht 1999 (n=254) is the only data point, and it is an informal practitioner survey [[interim-report-breed-prior-individual-vs-population]].
- No GPS-telemetry study exists for lost podencos or Ibizan hounds; the 5–15 km radius is mechanistic inference [[interim-report-podenco-lost-movement-pattern]].
- No direct empirical chase-fixation study exists for galgos; the OR=8.34 transfer from English racing greyhounds is a phylogenetic inference subject to the wide 1.72–42.38 confidence interval [[interim-report-galgo-vs-greyhound-behavioral-equivalence]].
- Normando 2024 does not provide a numerical prevalence beyond ">1 in 5"; the precise group-mean shift for galgo stranger-fear is unknown [[prevalence-of-some-behavioural-problems-in-two-groups-of-rescue-dogs-galgos-from]].
- The Normando 2025 sighthound study does not include galgo español or podenco specifically; within-type galgo-vs-greyhound differences in chase intensity remain unmeasured [[behavioural-characteristics-of-sighthounds-an-exploratory-investigation-dog-beha]].
- MacLean et al. 2019 (PNAS) — the strongest pro-breed-prior academic position — is paywalled and inaccessible in this corpus; the heritability of stranger-fear specifically within sighthounds is therefore not bounded from above [[interim-report-breed-prior-individual-vs-population]].
- Pet-abandoned vs. hunting-discarded galgo populations may differ behaviorally; Algarve population mix is unknown [[interim-report-galgo-vs-greyhound-behavioral-equivalence]].

### Practical Encoding

Encode breed via two channels rather than one. Channel A — *capture_strategy_prior* — is a discrete selector that maps breed_class to {passive_default, mixed, active_default}. Galgo → passive_default (no direct approach, conspecific lure preferred, trap from day 1). Podenco → passive_default with scent-anchor variant (familiar home items at trap site, not pure food). Beagle → mixed with scent-following extension (scout downwind of escape point along scent-trail vectors). Toy → mixed (small radius, owner-led search permitted). Herding / guardian → passive_default. Mixed unknown → mixed defaulting to active. Channel B — *probability_score* — uses breed as one input among many, capped at ≤10% weight, with individual behavioral signals (owner-reported stranger response, prior loss events, history of fearfulness) dominating from the first data point [[interim-report-breed-prior-individual-vs-population]]. The action gate is governed by Channel A, not Channel B: even if individual signals later suggest the galgo is more gregarious than baseline, the passive-only action gate remains until a high-λ camera-confirmed observation arrives. This asymmetry encodes the asymmetric error cost: a false gregarious classification kills the dog (fatal crowd convergence); a false xenophobic classification wastes trap time only. For the Algarve specifically, hard-code dual priors for galgo: a *spatial prior* anchored at prey-loss terminus when escape circumstance is prey-triggered (OR=8.34 displacement bias from the departure point toward the last visual/auditory contact with prey), and an *approach-avoidance prior* that holds independently of the spatial prior and disables active-approach options. For podenco, hard-code a *radially expanding scent-search* spatial prior (irregular spiral, 5–15 km radius in 48h, scrubland-biased) combined with a *food-lure-suppressed* arousal prior that delays trap baiting until camera evidence shows the dog returning to a settled feeding pattern.

## 3. Bayesian Updating from Sightings

### Key Empirical Findings

The lost-dog literature does not contain a peer-reviewed dog-specific sighting-update framework. The closest analogs are search-and-rescue (SAR) Bayesian methods developed for lost persons. Lin and Goodrich (2010) propose a Bayesian model for Wilderness Search and Rescue (WiSAR) that constructs a probability distribution map using publicly available terrain features and a first-order Markov transition matrix to produce temporal posterior predictive maps; the framework explicitly allows domain experts to encode uncertainty in priors and update posteriors as behavioral data are collected [[a-bayesian-approach-to-modeling-lost-person-behaviors-based-on-terrain-features]]. SARBayes extends this with a lognormal distance model fitted to International Search & Rescue Incident Database (ISRID) data; the MapScore evaluation framework gives the Distance Ring model a score of 0.78 (95% CI 0.74–0.82, n=376) and a Combined Distance+Watershed model a score of 0.81 (95% CI 0.77–0.84) [[sarbayes-bayesian-methods-for-wisar]]. Hashimoto et al. (2022) introduce the first time-discrete agent-based lost-person model validated against 65 ISRID hiker incidents, modeling lost-subject behavior as a six-element PMF (Random Walk, Route Travel, Direction Travel, Stay Put, View-Enhancing, Backtrack) drawn i.i.d. per time step; their fitted hiker profile is p_hiker = [0.055, 0.377, 0.559, 0.003, 0.006, 0], with leave-one-out cross-validation showing 58.5% of held-out incidents exceeding the 95th percentile of untrained distributions [[source-analysis-hashimoto-2022-agent-based-lost-person-model]]. Hashimoto's model produces the *forward* spatial prior; it does not include a posterior-update mechanism for incorporating sightings. The sighting-update layer must be built separately.

The MAR practitioner protocol provides the operational sighting framework. HARTT's intake schema specifies that every sighting must record date, exact time, exact location, direction of travel, and observer contact information [[lost-pet-owner-guide-hartt]] [[source-analysis-lost-pet-owner-guide-hartt]]. Direction of travel is the highest-value field — it tells the search where the dog is heading, not just where it was. Triangulation across two or three sightings produces a movement vector that is operationally more useful than any single sighting's position fix [[source-analysis-lost-pet-owner-guide-hartt]]. The MAR sighting-response protocol is unambiguous on the action layer: receive a sighting → send one calm person to place a food station within 50 feet of the sighting location → set up a trail camera → confirm regular visits over 2–3 days → set a humane trap with door tied open overnight initially → spring the trap only once the dog has been seen entering and eating consistently [[when-do-you-set-a-trap-for-a-lost-dog-missing-animal-response-network]] [[lure-lost-or-stray-dogs-do-not-chase-after-them-missing-animal-response-network]] [[stay-out-of-the-woods-missing-animal-response-network]]. At no step in this protocol is a crowd of volunteers introduced.

The action-gate principle is the operationally critical insight of the Bayesian sighting layer: *belief update and action are different decisions*. A sighting always updates the spatial probability map (regardless of reliability), but the action triggered by that sighting depends on temperament class and behavioral phase [[interim-report-bayesian-sighting-update]] [[interim-report-search-mode-temperament-sighting-protocol]]. For xenophobic, aloof, or evasion-phase dogs, the only action gate that fires on a sighting is "deploy single calm operator + camera station." For gregarious + exploratory dogs in Phase 1, the action gate may fire "coordinated quiet response." Crowd convergence is never authorized for any dog identified as fearful. The Princess Borzoi case illustrates the cost of violating this gate: repeated crowd-at-sighting events pushed her across two states (Wyoming → Colorado → Wyoming) and ultimately killed her on Highway 34 [[stay-out-of-the-woods-missing-animal-response-network]]. The Winnie case (puppy-mill rescue, 69 days missing) provides the dose-response anchor for crowd displacement: a single crowd convergence at her first sighting displaced her 7 miles in one hour [[dog-befriends-a-fox-while-lost-in-blizzard]]. CT Dog Gone Recovery's formal rule generalizes: never broadcast sighting coordinates publicly for any dog not confirmed as already in cooperative contact with a human at the sighting location [[interim-report-search-mode-temperament-sighting-protocol]].

Sighting reliability is the second operational pillar. The MAR community treats trail-camera observations as the gold standard — a camera image is the only acceptable trigger for trap deployment [[when-do-you-set-a-trap-for-a-lost-dog-missing-animal-response-network]]. Eyewitness sightings carry graded weight depending on observer quality (description specificity, ability to distinguish the dog from similar local dogs), distance and lighting (daylight at <30m more reliable than night at 100m), and corroboration (independent observers in similar locations). No peer-reviewed dog-specific reliability framework exists; eyewitness accuracy literature from human forensic psychology is the only adjacent calibrated body [[interim-report-bayesian-sighting-update]]. The Bayesian-update locus assigns operational λ weights: camera ≈ 0.95, clear daylight eyewitness with specific description ≈ 0.70, uncertain or night eyewitness ≈ 0.35, sighting embedded in or following crowd activity ≈ 0.20 [[interim-report-bayesian-sighting-update]].

Updating an expected-position distribution at time T+12h given a confirmed sighting at time T requires three components: a movement-kernel (how far and in what direction the dog typically moves in 12h given its phase and temperament), a terrain transition matrix (probability of movement across watershed, road, fence, scrub-vs-open boundaries), and the λ-weighted sighting likelihood. For a galgo prey-triggered escape with a confirmed sighting at scrubland coordinate X with direction-of-travel noted, the 12h posterior is concentrated within a 5–10 km elliptical region oriented along direction-of-travel from X, biased toward scrubland over open ground, with secondary mass at the prey-loss terminus (the 8.34× chase-fixation anchor) if a prey-stimulus location is known [[effects-of-lure-type-on-chase-related-behaviour-in-racing-greyhounds-pmc]] [[interim-report-galgo-vs-greyhound-behavioral-equivalence]]. For a podenco in active prey-drive, the 12h posterior is a radially expanding scrubland-biased pattern centered on X with high entropy (5–15 km plausible) [[interim-report-podenco-lost-movement-pattern]]. For a settled survival-mode dog (any breed) with established feeding patterns, the 12h posterior is a low-entropy distribution clustered near the known feeding station, because survival-mode dogs "form patterns" and "develop a routine" once they have located reliable food, water, and shelter [[understanding-survival-mode-behavior-in-lost-dogs-k9s-on-call-missing-pet-servic]] [[survival-mode-buddha-dog-rescue-recovery]].

The Lord 2007 vs. MAR-doctrine tension (active search empirically associated with recovery vs. active search empirically associated with mortality) resolves through temperament-gated routing. Lord 2007b's neighborhood-search recovery dominance (49% per Weiss 2012) is a population-mean that is dominated by gregarious dogs found in the first 24–48 hours; MAR doctrine is built on the hard-case sub-population (xenophobic, trauma-history, evasion-phase) that does not appear in Lord's sample at proportional rates [[interim-report-search-mode-temperament-sighting-protocol]] [[frequency-of-lost-dogs-and-cats-in-the-united-states-and-the-methods-used-to-loc]]. Both findings are valid for their populations; Nona disproportionately encounters the hard-case population because easy cases resolve before platform engagement. The engine must therefore implement the MAR-doctrine default, not the Lord-population default.

The interpretive beat: sightings are belief signals, not action permissions. The Bayesian engine should be designed as two layered modules — a continuous map updater that integrates every sighting weighted by λ, and a discrete action selector that reads the (temperament × phase × sighting-quality) tuple and authorizes a narrow set of permitted responses. Coupling these two modules is the most common engineering mistake (high-quality sighting → high-confidence map update → reflexive crowd response), and it is fatal in the literal Princess-Borzoi sense.

### Knowledge Gaps

- No peer-reviewed dog-specific Bayesian sighting-update framework exists; all current frameworks are adapted from WiSAR human SAR literature [[interim-report-bayesian-sighting-update]] [[sarbayes-bayesian-methods-for-wisar]].
- λ reliability weights for dog sightings are practitioner-derived inferences from adjacent (human-forensic) literature; no controlled study validates the specific 0.95 / 0.70 / 0.35 / 0.20 values [[interim-report-bayesian-sighting-update]].
- Hashimoto's PMF model is fitted for hikers, not dogs; the six behavioral strategies do not map perfectly to dog displacement (e.g., "view-enhancing" has no clear dog analog; "stay put" has different prior probability) [[source-analysis-hashimoto-2022-agent-based-lost-person-model]].
- Direction-of-travel reliability has not been quantified for eyewitness reports of dogs; the practitioner consensus that "direction is the most important field" is operational rather than measured.
- Crowd-displacement dose-response is anchored on a single quantified case (Winnie); no controlled or systematic data exists across multiple cases.
- HARTT's 70–80% within-1-mile heuristic is presented without sample size or methodology; it is internally inconsistent across HARTT materials [[source-analysis-lost-pet-owner-guide-hartt]].

### Practical Encoding

Implement the sighting layer as two decoupled modules. Module A (belief) maintains a continuous spatial probability distribution P(location | sightings, time, temperament, breed). Each sighting updates P via a Bayesian step where the likelihood term is the sighting's λ value times the movement-kernel evaluated at the sighting location given the prior, and the prior decays over time according to the temperament-conditional movement kernel (galgo 5–10 km/12h prey-triggered; podenco 5–15 km/12h prey-drive; settled survival <1 km/12h; gregarious early-phase 0–2 km/12h). Module B (action) reads (temperament_class, phase_distribution, latest_sighting_λ, prior_failed_approaches) and authorizes one of {direct_approach_owner, single_calm_operator, camera_station_only, full_lockdown}. The action gate is non-bypassable by belief confidence: even λ=0.95 trail-camera evidence of an apparently friendly galgo does not authorize crowd response. The minimum trap-deployment trigger is camera-confirmed feeding pattern over 48 hours at one specific station [[when-do-you-set-a-trap-for-a-lost-dog-missing-animal-response-network]]. Sighting intake forms must enforce the five HARTT fields (date, time, location, direction-of-travel, observer contact) [[source-analysis-lost-pet-owner-guide-hartt]]. Public sighting broadcast is gated by a binary classifier: permitted only when temperament_class = gregarious AND phase = exploratory AND the sighting reports the dog as already approaching or accepting human contact. All other cases route the sighting to the case coordinator only. The system should log every action-gate decision for retrospective analysis, building toward a dog-specific dataset that can replace the SARBayes/Hashimoto human-derived priors with empirically fitted dog priors over time.

## 4. Fear Escalation Dynamics

### Key Empirical Findings

Fear escalation in lost dogs is the dominant pathway to recovery failure and death. Lost Dogs of America states from thousands of cases over 13 years that the top three causes of death in lost dogs are vehicle strike, train strike, and drowning — and all three are "directly linked to pressure from searchers" [[never-follow-chase-or-pressure-a-lost-dog-lost-dogs-of-america]]. The mechanism is well-documented across MAR-aligned sources: a dog in flight mode perceives any approaching human as a predator, including its owner; "every human is viewed as a predator – even the one that has fed, loved, walked, bathed and pampered them for the last 2-4-12 years" [[survival-mode-buddha-dog-rescue-recovery]]. Stay Out of the Woods (MAR) describes the cortisol mechanism that produces this perceptual inversion: peak HPA activation floods the brain with glucocorticoids, the sympathetic nervous system temporarily blocks memory access, and "the dog doesn't recognize owner/guardian until parasympathetic system reengages via calming" [[stay-out-of-the-woods-missing-animal-response-network]]. The What You Don't Know About Lost Pets source (Albrecht-Thiessen, Maddie's Fund) is more specific: "the olfactory portion of a dog's brain closes down during the 'fight or flight' process and a panicked dog likely won't recognize their guardian's scent" [[what-you-dont-know-about-lost-pets-can-hurt-them]]. Olfactory shutdown during peak cortisol is the load-bearing mechanism: it explains why familiar scent items (owner's worn clothing, the dog's bedding) sometimes fail as recovery lures during the acute phase but succeed later as the parasympathetic system re-engages [[understanding-survival-mode-behavior-in-lost-dogs-k9s-on-call-missing-pet-servic]].

The "never chase, never call" rule has a specific neurobiological mechanism. Kat Albrecht's MAR case database documents named cases — Lucy (Case 10-264, Boston Terrier; Lacey (Case 10-267, Pomeranian); KoKo — where prior failed capture attempts by strangers conditioned the dog's name itself into a flight trigger. Per the Don't Call Your Dog page: "if too many people have already tried to capture the dog, calling him becomes a 'trigger' that can cause him to automatically take off in fear when anyone, including his owner, calls him" [[dont-call-your-dog-missing-animal-response-network]] [[never-call-a-lost-dog-missing-animal-response-network]]. The Never Call A Lost Dog page provides the full chain: dogs flooded with adrenaline "create associations... the first person who calls or whistles — if the dog is in fight or flight mode — may cause him to panic and bolt whenever he hears those signals, even if it's his owner" [[never-call-a-lost-dog-missing-animal-response-network]]. The Lucy case resolved in 4 days through neon posters plus strict no-calling protocol; a neighbor's failed-call attempt was the conditioning event. The Lacey case resolved when the owner sat down and *sang* (specifically, Elvis songs) — a non-conditioned sound signature unique to the owner — rather than calling the dog's name [[never-call-a-lost-dog-missing-animal-response-network]].

The three triggers for blind-panic / immediate evasion-mode onset are documented in MAR: (1) xenophobic / fearful temperament baseline, (2) loud noises (fireworks, gunfire, thunder), (3) traumatic incident (vehicle accident, explosion, severe pursuit) [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]]. These produce "the most difficult to catch" dogs that "travel far, travel fast, and avoid human contact, even with their own family members" [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]]. The MAR taxonomy distinguishes three temperaments — Gregarious, Aloof, Xenophobic — and three escape causes — Opportunistic Journey, Wanderlust, Blind Panic. The intersection of xenophobic temperament with blind-panic trigger produces the most extreme behavior: instant Phase 2 entry, near-zero approach probability, long-distance displacement, high mortality risk from traffic.

The behavioral signals that distinguish evasion mode from approachable mode are well-cataloged. Pet FBI's three-zone framework identifies Awareness Zone (dog detects human presence without alarm), Alert Zone (posture change, dilated pupils, weight shift), and Action Zone (threshold for bolting); approach must remain in Awareness — typically 45 degrees offset, never head-on [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]]. The Whole Dog Journal protocol catalogues approach signals (dog lowers head to sniff toward observer, ear position relaxes, tail loosens, body weight forward) and evasion signals (ears flatten, tail tucks, body weight shifts to rear, fixed stare at escape route) [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]]. Greyhound-specific signals add the freeze response — "frozen or stiff posture, pupils dilated, trembling, tense closed mouth, ears back, tail tucked, body hunched, all feet stuck to floor, leaning backwards" — which is recoverable because the dog is visible and not actively moving; this contrasts with the flight-dominant response seen in trauma-rescued galgos, where the same internal state produces active escape rather than freeze [[2-fear-and-anxiety-greyhounds-as-pets]] [[interim-report-galgo-vs-greyhound-behavioral-equivalence]] [[sos-galgos-adopt-a-survivor]]. K9s On Call lists the evasion-mode behavioral cluster: nocturnal activity cycle, daytime hiding under structures or in woods or drainage pipes, extreme silence (no barking), pattern formation around safe hiding spots and food sources, water-source seeking [[understanding-survival-mode-behavior-in-lost-dogs-k9s-on-call-missing-pet-servic]].

When active searching becomes counterproductive is the most important operational question in this section. Lost Dogs of America's framing is unambiguous: "Lost dogs that are being pressured will stay in 'panic' mode and will make very poor decisions, whereas lost dogs who aren't being pursued or pressured will make very wise decisions and may survive indefinitely" [[never-follow-chase-or-pressure-a-lost-dog-lost-dogs-of-america]]. The empirical anchors for recovery-failure-from-incorrect-owner-behavior are the Princess Borzoi case (repeated crowd-at-sighting events caused multi-state displacement and ultimately a fatal vehicle strike on Highway 34 in Colorado) [[stay-out-of-the-woods-missing-animal-response-network]] and the Winnie case (single crowd convergence at first sighting → 7 miles in one hour displacement; ultimately recovered through 69-day passive protocol using a magnet-dog approach) [[dog-befriends-a-fox-while-lost-in-blizzard]]. Shy Lost Dog Strategies (Lost Dogs of America) elaborates the shy-dog protocol: hidden dogs in the first hours and days are *safe* (not at risk from cars, overheating, or drowning while stationary) and the worst thing well-meaning neighbors can do is over-feed the dog from multiple sources, undermining the hunger gradient that draws the dog to the humane trap [[shy-lost-dog-strategies-lost-dogs-of-america]]. The Lure Lost or Stray Dogs page formalizes the protocol: "For lost dogs it is FIGHT or FLIGHT, most will choose flight. They are going to run, they do not take the time and say to themselves you're their Mom or Dad, but to them at that moment, you are a PREDATOR" [[lure-lost-or-stray-dogs-do-not-chase-after-them-missing-animal-response-network]]. Posters and flyers should keep information "vague enough to verify sightings (don't give full location — prevents well-meaning people from mobbing the area and pushing the dog further)" [[lure-lost-or-stray-dogs-do-not-chase-after-them-missing-animal-response-network]].

The interpretive beat: fear escalation is monotonic and adrenaline-conditioned. Each failed capture attempt does not just fail to recover the dog; it actively raises the threshold for any future capture attempt. The system must be designed to *prevent* fear escalation, not merely respond to it after it occurs. This means the action gate must be conservative by default (passive_only, single calm operator) and only relax to active search when temperament_class, phase, and prior_failed_approaches all permit it.

### Calibration Numbers

- **Top causes of lost-dog death** (Lost Dogs of America, thousands of cases, 13 years): vehicle strike (#1), train strike (#2), drowning (#3) — all attributable to search pressure [[never-follow-chase-or-pressure-a-lost-dog-lost-dogs-of-america]] [[lost-dog-behavior-tips-lost-dogs-illinois]].
- **Flight response prevalence under arousal**: ≥95% of survival-mode dogs respond to stimuli with flight rather than fight [[survival-mode-buddha-dog-rescue-recovery]].
- **Olfactory shutdown threshold**: occurs during peak cortisol / acute flight; familiar scent fails until parasympathetic re-engagement [[what-you-dont-know-about-lost-pets-can-hurt-them]] [[stay-out-of-the-woods-missing-animal-response-network]].
- **Crowd-displacement dose**: 7 miles per crowd convergence event (Winnie); cumulative multi-event displacement can span states (Princess Borzoi) [[dog-befriends-a-fox-while-lost-in-blizzard]] [[stay-out-of-the-woods-missing-animal-response-network]].
- **Three blind-panic triggers**: xenophobic temperament (innate baseline), loud noises (fireworks / gunfire / thunder), traumatic incident (vehicle accident, explosion, severe pursuit) [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]].
- **Name-conditioning threshold**: undefined empirically, but documented as triggered by even a single failed capture attempt where the dog's name was called during flight (Lucy 10-264, Lacey 10-267) [[never-call-a-lost-dog-missing-animal-response-network]] [[dont-call-your-dog-missing-animal-response-network]].
- **Approach-zone geometry**: 45° offset, not head-on; remain in Awareness Zone (dog detects observer without posture change) [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]].
- **Evasion-mode behavioral cluster**: nocturnal activity, daytime hiding, silence (no barking), pattern formation around food/water/shelter, weight on hindquarters with fixed stare at escape route, ears flat, tail tucked [[understanding-survival-mode-behavior-in-lost-dogs-k9s-on-call-missing-pet-servic]] [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]].
- **Approachable-mode behavioral cluster**: ears neutral, tail loose, weight forward, head lowered to sniff toward observer, willingness to make brief eye contact [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]].
- **Galgo flight-vs-freeze ratio**: rescued galgos display flight-dominant responses (multiple individual SOS Galgos profiles); English racing greyhounds display freeze-dominant responses (Greyhounds as Pets NZ) [[sos-galgos-adopt-a-survivor]] [[2-fear-and-anxiety-greyhounds-as-pets]] [[interim-report-galgo-vs-greyhound-behavioral-equivalence]].
- **Shy-dog hiding safety window**: hours to days of hiding without movement is *safe*, not dangerous; intervention to "rescue" a hiding dog is the danger [[shy-lost-dog-strategies-lost-dogs-of-america]].

### Knowledge Gaps

- The exact number of failed capture attempts that conditions name-calling into a permanent flight trigger is not measured; MAR cases document it occurring after a single failed attempt in some cases but not others [[never-call-a-lost-dog-missing-animal-response-network]].
- Reversibility of adrenaline conditioning is not documented quantitatively. Whether a name-conditioned dog can be re-paired with a non-aversive recovery signal (Lacey's owner singing Elvis) reliably enough to encode as a protocol is anecdotal [[never-call-a-lost-dog-missing-animal-response-network]].
- Direct measurement of cortisol in free-ranging lost dogs (vs. shelter-confined dogs in Hennessy's data) does not exist.
- The dose-response curve for crowd-size vs. displacement-distance has only single-point anchors (Winnie 7 mi from one crowd event); the slope is unknown.
- Heat / Algarve-summer interaction with fear escalation is unmeasured — dehydration likely lowers tolerance for additional stress, but no data quantifies this.
- The shift point between approachable-mode and evasion-mode in specific dogs is genuinely unpredictable per Buddha Dog and Holiday Barn: "very little correlation with previous behavior/life history" [[survival-mode-buddha-dog-rescue-recovery]] [[interim-report-dog-phase-transition-thresholds]].
- The Lord 2007b active-search recovery data is biased toward dogs that successfully recovered — the dogs that died from search pressure are absent from the recovered sample [[interim-report-search-mode-temperament-sighting-protocol]].

### Practical Encoding

Treat fear escalation as a state machine with monotonic transitions. Default initial state for all dogs is *unconditioned*. The state advances to *adrenaline-primed* when any one of the three blind-panic triggers (xenophobic baseline; loud-noise event; traumatic incident) is reported at intake. It advances to *name-conditioned* whenever any prior failed approach attempt is logged where the dog's name was called during the dog's flight; this transition is currently treated as irreversible by the engine, with the explicit understanding that practitioner anecdote supports partial reversibility via non-conditioned signal substitution (Lacey/Elvis pattern). It advances to *crowd-conditioned* whenever a crowd-convergence event is logged at any sighting. Each state advancement tightens the action gate: unconditioned dogs in Phase 1 can be approached by the owner directly; adrenaline-primed dogs require calming-signal protocol (45° approach, lowered posture, lip-smacking, food drop without reaching); name-conditioned dogs require the owner to substitute a unique non-name sound signature (singing, whistling a unique melody, calling out a non-name word like the dog's favorite food); crowd-conditioned dogs are restricted to camera-station-only protocol regardless of who the operator is. The intake form must capture the three blind-panic trigger fields (temperament_xenophobic_yes/no, loud_noise_event_at_loss_yes/no, traumatic_incident_at_loss_yes/no). The sighting form must capture whether the dog's name was called by anyone during the encounter and whether a crowd of more than three people was present in the sighting area within the prior 24 hours. The system must surface the current fear-escalation state to the owner and operators at every step, so that protocol selections are visibly tied to the state. Crucially, the system must implement an override-protection: an owner whose dog is in name-conditioned or crowd-conditioned state must be prevented from broadcasting sighting locations publicly, and must be coached actively away from calling the dog's name even when distressed. For Algarve-specific deployment, hard-code an additional state — *summer-heat-elevated* — that fires whenever ambient temperature exceeds 30°C; this state shortens trap-monitoring intervals, prioritizes water-station deployment alongside food-station deployment, and accelerates the urgency multiplier on the alert prioritization function. Looking forward, as Nona accumulates Algarve-specific case data, the engine should learn dog-specific fear-escalation transition probabilities and replace the current static state machine with an empirically fitted Bayesian state-transition model — but only after sufficient case volume (likely n>200 cases) to support reliable estimation.

The forward-looking implication of fear-escalation dynamics for Nona's overall design is that the platform's primary product is not "finding lost dogs" but "preventing the fear escalation that turns recoverable dogs into permanently lost dogs." Every design decision — intake question phrasing, sighting-form fields, push-notification copy, owner coaching scripts, volunteer routing rules — should be evaluated against the criterion of whether it reduces or increases the probability of crowd convergence, name-calling during flight, or unauthorized owner pursuit. In the medium term, an effective Nona deployment in the Algarve will reduce vehicle-strike, train-strike, and drowning mortality among lost dogs by routing well-meaning bystander energy *away* from the search area and into the disciplined passive protocol (food stations, cameras, traps, magnet dogs, conspecific lures) that the evidence base supports. The galgo and podenco populations of the Algarve, with their elevated trauma baselines and prey-drive ranging respectively, stand to benefit disproportionately because they are precisely the populations for which the conventional active-search default is most lethal.
