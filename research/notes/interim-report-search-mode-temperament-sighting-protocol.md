# Interim report: search-mode-temperament-sighting-protocol

**Locus question:** For which temperament/phase combinations does active searching increase vs. decrease recovery probability — and when a sighting arrives, what is the precise decision rule distinguishing safe individual response from dangerous crowd-broadcast response?
**Flavor:** dialectical

## What the corpus already said

The corpus established the foundational behavioral typology (Albrecht/MAR: gregarious / aloof / xenophobic) and the three root causes of displacement (opportunistic journey, wanderlust, blind panic) [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network]]. It documented the empirical tension at the heart of this locus: Lord 2007b (n=187 dogs, Montgomery County OH) found that calling/visiting animal agencies (34.8%) and neighborhood signs (15.2%) drove recovery, with "neighborhood search" prominent in Weiss 2012 (49% recovery method); yet MAR doctrine and practitioner case records show that active searching — especially group searching — triggers fatal displacement events [[lost-pet-statistics-lost-pet-research-and-recovery]]. The Winnie case quantified the displacement cost: a crowd of well-meaning helpers caused a xenophobic dog to bolt 7 miles in one hour [[dog-befriends-a-fox-while-lost-in-blizzard]]. The Lucy and Lacey Boston Terrier/Pomeranian cases documented adrenaline-conditioning: other people's calling attempts create a conditioned flight trigger that fires even for the owner's voice [[never-call-a-lost-dog-missing-animal-response-network]]. The CT Dog Gone Recovery note explicitly identifies public social-media sighting broadcasts as an evasion-escalation trigger [[behaviors-to-avoid-ct-dog-gone-recovery]]. The shy-dog protocol from Pobloskie (Lost Dogs of America) established that hiding in the first few days post-flight is safe and that starvation is a prerequisite for successful trapping [[shy-lost-dog-strategies-lost-dogs-of-america]].

## What the new sources say

### dont-call-your-dog-missing-animal-response-network

The MAR "Don't Call Your Dog" page provides the physiological mechanism behind the never-call rule and the formal approach protocol. The key passage: *"There's a reason why one of the worst things that you can do is call out to a stray dog or panicked dog. The reason is that it's likely that other people (who encountered the loose dog) have already tried to capture him and calling him has become a 'trigger' that causes him to automatically bolt in fear when anyone, including his owner, calls him."* Critically, the protocol adds that during peak adrenaline state, **olfactory processing shuts down** — the owner's scent cannot be detected by the dog until cortisol drops enough for the parasympathetic system to re-engage. This means even food lures fail at peak panic. The prescribed calming signal sequence is: (1) get low/flat to ground; (2) avoid direct eye contact; (3) face sideways, not predator-straight-on; (4) make lip-smacking sounds with crinkly food packaging; (5) drop food on ground without reaching; (6) SING if other methods have been used by prior failed-capturers (unique sound signature not yet conditioned as a threat trigger). The Murphy military-owner video case is cited: owner's calling and direct gaze triggered terror; removing hat and switching to indirect approach allowed recognition only after calming.

### snap-into-action-missing-animal-response-network

The "Snap Into Action" page operationalizes the magnet-dog method as an alternative to food luring for xenophobic dogs. The key quote: *"Kody is wiggly, friendly, and gives off play signals to other dogs. We use magnet dogs to draw in panicked strays that may be leary of people but that will zoom in right up to our magnet dog. They get tunnel vision and never notice us because they're so focused on making eye contact with the magnet dog."* This is the mechanism Winnie's recovery ultimately used (Phoebe, the familiar rescue dog). The note establishes the formal two-track approach protocol: food lure for dogs that respond to food; magnet dog + snappy snare for dogs whose arousal is food-suppressed but social-approach-capable. Important operational note: "We never know which 'bait' a dog will respond to (food or other dogs)" — meaning triage is required at the sighting.

### when-do-you-set-a-trap-for-a-lost-dog-missing-animal-response-network

This page contains the operationally precise trap-deployment trigger rule: *"The short and simple answer is when you have the dog eating on a trail camera in a specific location."* The sequential protocol is: (1) place familiar scent article near escape point; (2) establish food station with smelly canned food with gravy; (3) confirm dog eating consistently on trail camera at that specific location; (4) obtain landowner permission if off-property; (5) set humane trap with 1-3 hour monitoring intervals. The note also distinguishes gregarious dogs (can be lured without traps) from xenophobic dogs (require food-station conditioning + trap). The critical finding for sighting management: "The dog will not travel long distances just because you set a trap in a specific place" — meaning sighting data must be used to identify current territory center, not escape-point anchor. A further timing rule emerges: trap doors should be tied open overnight (bungee cord) to allow the dog to enter freely and build comfort, then spring only once the dog is eating regularly. This implies a minimum multi-day food-station phase before trap deployment.

### survival-mode-buddha-dog-rescue-recovery

The Buddha Dog Rescue guide adds quantitative framing to survival-mode onset probability: *"Depending on your dog's history, there is a 50-75-90-99% chance that your dog, being lost, is not exhibiting the same behaviors you would normally expect."* The range is significant — it means baseline gregarious dogs have some probability of remaining approachable (50% chance of behavioral regression), while known-fearful, puppy-mill, or trauma-history dogs approach near-certainty of evasion behavior (90-99%). The guide adds a paradox not in other sources: *"oftentimes, when a dog in survival mode sees, hears or smells something familiar to them, they will leave the area and avoid it, creating a negative connotation to that spot."* This means owner presence at a sighting zone can actually anchor negative associations and drive the dog off the territory entirely — the opposite of owner-presence being comforting. Also documents that certain breeds/personalities are more prone to immediate transition to survival mode versus gradual onset over days/weeks.

### understanding-survival-mode-behavior-in-lost-dogs-k9s-on-call-missing-pet-servic

This K9s-on-call guide adds behavioral indicators of survival mode that distinguish it from the exploratory/wandering phase: nocturnal activity cycle (moving at night, hiding during day under porches/in woods/drainage pipes); extreme silence (no barking to avoid detection); pattern formation around safe hiding spots and food sources; water source seeking. This gives the behavioral signals needed for phase classification from sighting reports.

### lure-lost-or-stray-dogs-do-not-chase-after-them-missing-animal-response-network

This MAR guide provides the key framing for the search-vs-passive decision: *"For lost dogs it is FIGHT or FLIGHT, most will choose flight. They are going to run, they do not take the time and say to themselves you're their Mom or Dad, but to them at that moment, you are a PREDATOR."* The poster/flyer strategy note here is critical for sighting management: keep information "vague enough to verify sightings (don't give full location — prevents well-meaning people from mobbing the area and pushing the dog further)." This is the formal MAR policy against public sighting broadcast.

## Evidence synthesis

### Resolving the Lord 2007 vs. MAR paradox

The apparent contradiction — Lord 2007b shows 49% neighborhood-search recovery while MAR says active searching kills panicked dogs — dissolves when temperament × phase is applied. Lord 2007b's sample is biased toward dogs that ultimately survived and were reported to agencies or newspapers. The sample disproportionately captures gregarious dogs (found quickly by neighbors before evasion mode could develop) and aloof dogs (found through sustained search). The xenophobic dogs that died from pressure or disappeared permanently are underrepresented because their owners either stopped searching or the dogs were never recovered at all. Weiss 2012's random-sample methodology is less biased and shows higher ID-based recovery rates, suggesting the 49% neighborhood-search figure includes a large fraction of gregarious-dog recoveries (which inflates the neighborhood-search category). MAR doctrine, informed by thousands of non-survivorship-biased cases (including fatal outcomes like Princess the Borzoi, the unnamed dog hit by a train), corrects for this survivorship bias.

### Position A: Active neighborhood searching is the dominant recovery method

Weiss 2012 (n=1,015 households, random digit dial, nationally representative) and Lord 2007b (n=187, Ohio) both find neighborhood search as the plurality or majority recovery mechanism for dogs. These are published, peer-reviewed studies. MAR doctrine is expert practitioner opinion without a comparative trial design. For gregarious dogs in early phase (first 24-48 hours, pre-evasion-conditioning), active searching clearly works — the dog is not yet in flight mode and may approach any friendly human. The Lord data on median recovery time of 2 days (range 0.5-21) supports the view that most dogs are found quickly through active methods before survival mode solidifies.

**Best evidence for Position A:** Lord 2007b (n=187, empirical), Weiss 2012 (nationally representative sample). The 49% neighborhood-search figure is robust for the population-level question of how dogs are recovered overall.

### Position B: Active searching (especially group-based) increases mortality risk for panicked/xenophobic dogs and must be prohibited during the settling phase

The MAR-aligned sources document specific fatal and near-fatal outcomes from active searching of dogs already in evasion mode: Princess the Borzoi displaced from Wyoming to Colorado and back, killed on highway 34; unnamed dog at boarding facility pushed out of woods, killed by train; Lucy Boston Terrier conditioned to flee by neighborhood callers; Winnie displaced 7 miles in 1 hour by crowd convergence. The mechanism is established by neuroscience: elevated cortisol blocks olfactory processing and triggers sympathetic nervous system override, making the dog behaviorally indistinguishable from a wild prey animal regardless of training history. The adrenaline-conditioning effect means each failed capture attempt adds a new threat-trigger, compounding the problem over time.

**Best evidence for Position B:** MARN case documentation (multiple named cases); MAR protocol backed by SAR methodology; Buddha Dog quantification (50-99% behavioral regression depending on dog history); K9s-on-call behavioral indicator list (nocturnal cycle, silence, hiding during daytime).

### Resolution: Temperament × Phase Matrix

The evidence is simultaneously correct for different subpopulations:

| Temperament | Phase: First 24-48h (pre-conditioning) | Phase: Evasion-conditioned (post-pressure) |
|-------------|----------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| **Gregarious** | Active search: RECOMMENDED — dog will approach friendly humans | Active search: Still safe, but introduce calming signals first; direct approach fine |
| **Aloof** | Passive search (posters/door-to-door info gathering): RECOMMENDED; active approach counterproductive | Passive approach only; food station + trap cycle; extended timeline (weeks) |
| **Xenophobic / trauma-history** | Immediate passive protocol: NO active searching; food station within 24h of escape; owner presence at escape point only | Full evasion-mode protocol: no owner or searcher presence; remote monitoring only; familiar-dog lure or trap |

Phase is diagnosed from behavioral signals in sightings: if dog is stationary/hiding during daytime, moving at night, silent, and established a territory (multiple sightings within 0.5-mile radius over 2+ days) → evasion mode. If dog is moving, visible during day, reactive to strangers (approaches or allows approach without bolting) → exploratory/settling phase.

### Sighting triage — the crowd-broadcast decision rule

The corpus is fully unified on this point (MAR, CTDGRV, Buddha Dog, K9s-on-call all agree): **public broadcast of sighting location coordinates is never appropriate for aloof/xenophobic dogs or any dog in confirmed evasion mode.** The mechanism is: broadcast triggers crowd convergence → crowd calls/approaches → dog flees territory → new territory center unknown → recovery timeline reset. The single exception where broadcast is safe: gregarious dog, exploratory phase, sighting confirms dog is stationary with people (already being fed by a family who found it). In that case the dog is not in evasion mode and the "finder" is the recovery asset.

CT Dog Gone Recovery provides the formal rule explicitly: *"DGR recommends the owner to tell everyone to share any sighting information directly to the owner or contact person and refrain from posting the dog's whereabouts on the internet."* MAR's poster-vagueness rule formalizes this at the initial marketing phase: do not give full location details even on recovery posters.

## Committed position

The Lord 2007 / Weiss 2012 data and the MAR doctrine are not in conflict — they are describing different dogs in different behavioral phases. The evidence across all sources, read together, produces a single coherent decision framework: active searching is recovery-positive for gregarious dogs in the first 24-72 hours (before fear-conditioning sets in), and recovery-negative for xenophobic/aloof dogs at any point and for any dog once fear-conditioning has been established by prior pursuit attempts. The sighting location broadcast decision follows a strict binary: private channel always for aloof/xenophobic/evasion-mode dogs (because crowd convergence produces measurable, lethal territory displacement — Winnie's 7 miles in one hour is the quantitative benchmark); public/semi-public channel acceptable only for gregarious dogs whose sighting confirms they are already in proximity to and accepting of human contact. Any OS built for lost dog rescue must operationalize this as a mandatory gate: before any sighting is shared with volunteers or social media, the system must classify the dog's temperament and current behavioral phase from sighting quality signals (daytime vs. nocturnal, stationary vs. moving, approach vs. flight when seen), and route sighting data accordingly — individual coordinator only for evasion-mode dogs, controlled volunteer response for gregarious explorers.

**Position:** Active searching is recovery-positive for gregarious dogs in the first 24-72 hours before fear-conditioning; it is recovery-negative (potentially fatal) for xenophobic, trauma-history, or fear-triggered dogs at any phase and for any dog once evasion-conditioning has been established by prior approach attempts. Sighting location must be routed to search coordinator only (never broadcast publicly) whenever the dog is classified as aloof/xenophobic or any sighting shows daytime hiding, nocturnal movement, or flight-on-approach behavior.

**Confidence:** High (>80%) for the core temperament-gated recommendation; medium (60-70%) for the specific 24-72h window as the gregarious/exploratory phase ceiling, because no published study tracks the exact onset-time of fear-conditioning after first approach attempts.

**Boundary conditions:** This applies to all lost dogs, but the stakes are highest for xenophobic/trauma-history dogs in rural/semi-rural terrain (like Algarve wilderness). In dense urban environments with high population density, gregarious dogs are typically recovered within hours because strangers approach them first; the evasion-conditioning pathway rarely has time to activate. In sparse-population rural terrain, fear-conditioning can develop over days without being detected because the dog is simply not encountered, after which any ill-timed approach by a well-meaning searcher triggers displacement into fatal traffic zones.

**What would change this position:** A controlled study (unlikely due to ethics) or large-N case database that tracked search method × temperament × outcome would be required to refine the 24-72h phase-transition window. If such a study showed that even gregarious dogs frequently enter evasion mode within 4-6 hours of first failed approach, the active-search window for gregarious dogs would shrink. If it showed that xenophobic dogs in exploratory phase (first 2-4 hours) can be safely approached by a single calm individual using calming signals, the "no active search for xenophobic dogs" rule would have a narrow early-phase exception. Neither refinement would change the core sighting-broadcast decision rule, which the evidence fully supports.

**Evidence weight:** 2 peer-reviewed empirical studies (Lord 2007b n=187, Weiss 2012 n=1015) support active search as population-level recovery mechanism; 5+ practitioner case records with named dogs and documented fatal/near-fatal outcomes support the prohibition on pressure for evasion-mode dogs; 4 practitioner protocols (MAR, CTDGRV, Buddha Dog, K9s-on-call) unanimously endorse the private-channel sighting rule; 1 quantified displacement event (Winnie, 7 miles in 1 hour from crowd) provides the dose-response anchor. No sources support public broadcast of sighting location for non-gregarious dogs.

## Decision Tree (formal output)

```
WHEN A SIGHTING IS RECEIVED:
│
├── STEP 1: Classify temperament from dog history
│   ├── Gregarious (friendly with strangers at home)
│   ├── Aloof (wary of strangers but food-responsive)
│   └── Xenophobic / trauma-history (fearful, puppy-mill, PTSD)
│
├── STEP 2: Classify behavioral phase from sighting quality
│   ├── Exploratory phase indicators: moving during daytime, reactive/curious
│   │   (approaching, sniffing), not bolting from visual contact at distance
│   └── Evasion phase indicators: daytime hiding (under structures/in woods),
│       nocturnal movement, silent, established territory (2+ sightings within
│       0.5mi radius over 2+ days), bolt-on-approach or bolt-on-calling
│
├── STEP 3: Active search decision
│   ├── Gregarious + Exploratory → ACTIVE SEARCH: single calm individual,
│   │   calming signals, no calling, curved approach. Small search party OK.
│   ├── Gregarious + Evasion → PASSIVE: food station + trail camera; individual
│   │   calming approach only; NO groups; owner may be present at escape point
│   ├── Aloof + any phase → PASSIVE: food station + trail camera; no active
│   │   searching; trap deployment after camera confirmation of feeding
│   └── Xenophobic + any phase → FULL PASSIVE: no owner/searcher in territory;
│       food station at greatest safe distance; remote monitoring only;
│       magnet-dog or familiar-dog lure when dog is calm-settled (weeks)
│
└── STEP 4: Sighting location broadcast decision
    ├── Gregarious + Exploratory + dog stationary with finder
    │   → SEMI-PUBLIC: share with identified volunteers who confirm calming-
    │     signal compliance; no open social media broadcast
    ├── Gregarious + Evasion → PRIVATE: coordinator + max 2 individuals only
    ├── Aloof + any phase → PRIVATE: coordinator only
    └── Xenophobic + any phase → STRICTLY PRIVATE: coordinator only;
        do NOT share with any volunteers who will physically approach;
        area-lockdown: instruct all known contacts in area to go indoors,
        cease calling, cease driving near territory
```

**Active search → passive transition trigger:**
Any sighting that shows bolt-on-approach or bolt-on-calling by ANY person (including owner) → immediately transition to passive protocol regardless of prior classification. Fear-conditioning is now established.

**Trap deployment trigger (MAR formal rule):**
Do not set trap until dog confirmed eating consistently at specific food station location on trail camera. Minimum 2-3 consecutive days eating at same location before trap deployment. Trap door open overnight (building comfort); spring during daytime hours when dog is in territory.

## Open questions

- No published study has tracked the exact onset time of fear-conditioning after first pursuit attempts for different temperament classes. The 24-72h window for gregarious dogs is inferred from median recovery time (Lord 2007b: 2 days) rather than behavioral observation.
- The Buddha Dog "50-75-90-99% regression probability" range lacks a published validation study; it appears to be practitioner calibration, not empirical measurement.
- No data exists on whether prior fear-conditioning fully resets once a dog has settled in a stable territory for 7+ days — does a dog in stable evasion mode become partially re-approachable over time, or does conditioning persist?
- The Algarve-specific context (sparse population, Podenco/Galgo prevalence, heat stress forcing daytime hiding) means evasion-phase indicators may be confounded by heat-driven daytime inactivity in gregarious dogs.
