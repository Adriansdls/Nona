What is a Galgo?
The Galgo Español, or Spanish greyhound, is an ancient breed and a member of the sighthound family.
Despite the name “greyhound,” the Spanish galgo is not closely related to the English or Irish Greyhound; the two breeds developed along separate lineages. In earlier centuries, galgos were highly prized, yet today many are treated as disposable hunting tools. GDS believes that galgos are an important part of Spain’s cultural heritage and deserve to be restored to the place of respect they once held.
Galgos may be smooth- or rough-coated and come in a wide variety of colors, including solid shades, brindle, or two-color combinations. Although they resemble racing greyhounds, their conformation is distinctly different. Galgos have leaner musculature, more characteristic of endurance runners than the sprinter’s build of the English or Irish racing greyhound. They are long, lean, and elegant dogs—so much so that it is difficult to describe them without overusing the word “long.” They typically have long backs, long tails, and streamlined heads with almond-shaped eyes.
In temperament, galgos share many traits with greyhounds: they are often calm, gentle, quiet, and laid-back. At the same time, they are curious and surprisingly athletic jumpers, meaning baby gates and low fences (such as four-foot barriers) are often not sufficient to contain them. Sometimes described as “40-mph couch potatoes,” galgos are happy to lounge for much of the day, content with a couple of short daily walks, though many can also make excellent jogging companions.
Many galgos live peacefully with cats and other small animals, while others—given careful, gradual introductions—can learn to do so once they understand appropriate boundaries.
In rural areas of Spain, galgos are widely used for hunting and hare coursing, often associated with betting. Because they are frequently regarded as expendable working animals, many are abandoned at the end of the short hunting season each year, and others suffer severe mistreatment when they are no longer considered useful.
Some GDS galgos have been rescued from perreras (municipal shelters where euthanasia may occur), where their lives were at risk. Many more were found abandoned or living rough, scavenging to survive until captured by the GDS team—who at times face local resistance while working to save a breed too often considered without value.
GDS Rescue Missions
Rescuing galgos is a challenging, unpredictable, and often dangerous undertaking.
An abandoned galgo that has been living rough may need to be patiently observed and coaxed with food for weeks—or sometimes months—before allowing a human to come near. GDS has rescued galgos found in wells, lying injured in fields, abandoned in dumpsters, running terrified through traffic, giving birth on roadsides, or left to die in deserted breeding camps. Because sightings can occur at any time, the GDS rescue team remains on call around the clock.
