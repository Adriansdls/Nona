# Interim report: galgo-vs-greyhound-behavioral-equivalence

**Locus question:** Can English greyhound chase research (OR=8.34 last-known-location fixation after prey disappears) transfer directly to galgo behavioral priors, or does the galgo trauma baseline make it a distinct model requiring independent priors?
**Flavor:** dialectical

## What the corpus already said

The width sweep established two key pillars. First, Starling, Wilson & McGreevy (2020) [[effects-of-lure-type-on-chase-related-behaviour-in-racing-greyhounds-pmc]] found that English racing greyhounds fixated on the last visible lure location at OR=8.34 (95% CI 1.72–42.38, p=0.009) when the lure stopped and went silent — directly analogous to a rabbit disappearing into Algarve scrubland. The study characterized this as appetitive (internally driven) chase motivation that persists without prey capture, supported by wild canid success rates of 5–13%. Second, Normando et al. (2024) [[prevalence-of-some-behavioural-problems-in-two-groups-of-rescue-dogs-galgos-from]] documented that in n=410 rescue dogs (198 galgos, 212 others), galgos showed categorically elevated fear of non-cohabiting adult people (>1 in 5 galgos, p=0.009) that was absent in the comparison group — establishing a breed-specific human-avoidance prior. Qualitative galgo rescue sources [[sos-galgos-adopt-a-survivor]] [[what-is-a-galgo-galgos-del-sol]] [[galgo-espaol-animal-corner]] documented hide-in-corner behavior, flight from human approach on intake, and weeks-to-months approach-avoidance timelines. The loci-analyst flagged the OR=8.34 as likely transferable to galgo prey-fixation mechanics while signaling uncertainty about whether the approach-avoidance component would compound or override it.

## What the new sources say

**Starling, Spurrett & McGreevy (2020) PMC7341205** [[a-pilot-study-of-methods-for-evaluating-the-effects-of-arousal-and-emotional-val]] — The upstream pilot study that the OR=8.34 paper extended. N=525 racing greyhounds at three NSW racetracks. Key finding for this locus: "Increasing Eye Temp After had a negative association with performance (Effect = −0.173, p=0.027), as did increasing age." This establishes that arousal is measurable in racing greyhounds via infrared thermography and that higher post-race arousal (more stressed/frustrated dogs) perform worse. The mechanism is confirmed: kennelling itself is "probably a source of ongoing distress at race meets." This upstream study is relevant because it anchors the OR=8.34 finding in a broader arousal-and-frustration framework — greyhound chase fixation is not just a curiosity but a marker of a persistent arousal state that does not resolve without consummation.

**McLennan / Broseghini et al. (2024)** [[arousal-and-predatory-motor-patterns]] — Critical mediating source on the predatory motor sequence (PMS) in sighthounds specifically. Broseghini et al. 2024 (Applied Animal Behaviour Science) proposed a 4-stage ethogram: Search > Approach > Chase > Bite. The McLennan synthesis states explicitly: "NOT seen in sighthounds in study (sighthounds don't search, they react)." This is the load-bearing distinction: sighthounds skip the Search stage and go directly to Orient > Eye-stalk > Chase when a stimulus appears. A galgo encountering a rabbit does not begin sniffing and searching — it reacts immediately. This compresses the time window between trigger and full-chase commitment to near zero, meaning the flight zone is oriented from the moment of first stimulus contact, not after deliberate approach.

**3 Lost Dogs Academy (Daybreak Dogs LLC)** [[predatory-motor-sequence-basics-3-lost-dogs-academy-daybreak-dogs-llc]] — Applied predatory motor sequence to lost-dog recovery context. Chase is confirmed as the highest-arousal stage, with arousal peaking "the instant before the dog catches (or thinks they're about to catch) their prey." If the sequence is interrupted (prey escapes, dog loses contact), arousal does not self-terminate — it remains elevated. This explains why a triggered galgo that loses a rabbit remains displaced and hypervigilant rather than self-correcting home.

**Normando, Bertomoro & Bonetti (2021) — J Vet Med Sci 83(4):566-572** [[efetchfcgi]] — Adoption satisfaction study, n=392 dogs (191 galgos). Key finding: "dog type, with being a galgo as a negative predictor" of owner satisfaction, specifically because behavioral problems in galgos affecting satisfaction differed from other dogs. Notably, "not being interested in social interactions with dogs" increased owner satisfaction in galgos (counterintuitive) while destructiveness decreased it. The study confirms that galgos display a distinct behavioral profile relative to other rescue dogs — including non-sighthound populations — in real-world adoption contexts.

**Minnesota Greyhound Rescue galgo profile** [[what-is-a-galgo-minnesota-greyhound-rescue]] — Practitioner rescue org documentation: galgos are "notoriously excellent jumpers" (minimum 5-foot fence required, 6-foot preferred), and "these dogs have almost universally seen maltreatment and neglect." Confirms universal trauma baseline across the Spanish galgo population, not a subset.

## Evidence synthesis

### Position A: Greyhound OR=8.34 transfers directly — galgo chase mechanics are equivalent

The argument for direct transfer rests on three pillars. First, evolutionary homology: galgo español and English greyhound share selective breeding for visual prey detection and chase over thousands of years, with documented crossbreeding events (early 20th century, English greyhound × galgo crosses for Spanish racing). Both breeds occupy FCI Group 10 (Sighthounds, Short-haired) and are functionally sighthound hunters. The predatory motor sequence architecture — react-and-chase rather than search-and-approach — is a structural feature of sighthound cognition documented across the family. Second, the OR=8.34 mechanism is not greyhound-specific in the sense of being breed-idiosyncratic; it describes a fundamental feature of predatory pursuit: when prey stimulus disappears (lure goes silent and stops on straight track), the animal fixates on the last-known-location of that stimulus. This is documented in wild canids as well (wolves, coyotes) and is a general property of pursuit predators. Third, Galgos del Sol rescue documentation [[what-is-a-galgo-galgos-del-sol]] confirms galgos are used for live hare coursing in rural Spain — they are working sighthound hunters who have pursued live prey in their operational environment, not just mechanical-lure athletes. The prey-fixation mechanism applies.

Strongest version of this position: the OR=8.34 should be applied to galgo chase displacement prediction because it describes a phylogenetically conserved and functionally observed prey-fixation mechanism in sighthounds, not an English greyhound racing peculiarity. When a galgo chasing a rabbit loses contact — rabbit enters scrub, burrow, or dense maquis — the galgo fixates on the prey-loss terminus. Search should be anchored to that point.

### Position B: Galgo trauma baseline creates a distinct behavioral model — greyhound data does not transfer for approach-avoidance

The argument against direct transfer is sharper than it first appears and applies to a different behavioral domain. Normando 2024 [[prevalence-of-some-behavioural-problems-in-two-groups-of-rescue-dogs-galgos-from]] establishes that rescued galgos display a qualitatively different fear-of-humans profile (>1 in 5, p=0.009) not present in other rescue dogs — including presumably other rescued sighthounds or greyhounds in the comparison group. The corpus's greyhound fear source [[2-fear-and-anxiety-greyhounds-as-pets]] documents the 4F stress response (Fidget/Freeze/Flight/Fight), with the freeze response "characteristic in greyhounds" — "frozen or stiff posture, pupils dilated, trembling, tense closed mouth, ears back, tail tucked." But rescued galgos display flight-dominant not freeze-dominant responses to human approach, as documented across 18+ individual intake profiles in [[sos-galgos-adopt-a-survivor]] (Dólar: "wanted to run away whenever a person approached"; Ludi: "would run straight to the corner whenever anyone approached"). This matters critically for lost-dog recovery: a freeze response makes a dog visible and catchable; a flight response makes them invisible and uncatchable.

The galgo trauma baseline is not a minor modifier — it is a categorical shift in the approach-avoidance system. SOS Galgos individual profiles, Galgos del Sol documentation ("weeks or sometimes months before allowing a human to come near"), and Animal Corner breed profile ("rescue galgos may come with considerable emotional and traumatic baggage") all converge: the lost galgo is operating in an environment where humans are threat signals, not resource signals. The 4F framework for greyhounds was built on Australian racing dogs transitioning to pet life — dogs with consistent positive human contact, structured feeding, and low trauma histories. The galgo rescue population has the opposite baseline.

Strongest version of this position: applying English greyhound approach-avoidance models to lost galgos will cause systematic underestimation of how far a galgo will flee from rescuers, how long it will remain in flight mode upon sighting humans, and how ineffective direct approach attempts are. The greyhound freeze response is a recoverable situation; the galgo flight response in a traumatized individual extends the search radius significantly.

## Evidence synthesis (continued)

The evidence supports a **partial transfer model** with domain-specific split. The two positions are not actually in conflict once the behavioral domain is properly separated:

1. **Chase mechanics (displacement from stimulus):** greyhound OR=8.34 data DOES transfer. The prey-fixation mechanism is phylogenetically conserved across sighthounds, documented in live-prey coursing contexts (not just mechanical lure racing), and consistent with the galgo's operational role as a coursing dog. The specific number should be applied with appropriate CI widening (source CI is already 1.72–42.38).

2. **Approach-avoidance mechanics (response to human recovery attempts):** greyhound data does NOT transfer. Normando 2024 (p=0.009, n=198 galgos) establishes that galgo human-avoidance is categorically distinct from other rescue dogs — and the greyhound fear literature is built on a racing/rehomed population with systematically lower trauma histories. The galgo operates under a survival-mode fear model (flight-dominant), not the freeze-dominant model seen in English greyhounds.

The predatory motor sequence source [[arousal-and-predatory-motor-patterns]] adds a third structural insight: because sighthounds skip the Search stage and go directly to Chase, the transition between "calm at home" and "full prey chase" is near-instantaneous. This means the displacement event itself is very fast and very far, but the post-displacement behavior — where the dog ends up and how it responds to recovery attempts — is governed by the trauma-fear system, not the chase system. Two different behavioral systems, two different evidence bases.

## Committed position

The greyhound OR=8.34 prey-fixation finding transfers to galgo chase displacement priors because it describes a phylogenetically conserved sighthound mechanism confirmed in live-prey coursing contexts, not a racing-dog idiosyncrasy; Nona should encode this as the primary prior for predicting where a triggered galgo will be found (prey-loss terminus, not departure point, with an 8x elevated probability relative to non-triggered dogs). However, all approach-avoidance priors — how a galgo responds to humans attempting recovery — must be built from galgo-specific evidence, because Normando 2024 (p=0.009, n=198) establishes that rescued galgos show categorically distinct human-fear profiles absent in other rescue dogs, and the galgo intake behavioral record shows flight-dominant (not freeze-dominant) responses that extend displacement under recovery pressure. The two-prior model is not a compromise: it is the only reading of the evidence that is internally consistent, because the chase mechanism and the fear-avoidance mechanism are governed by separate behavioral systems that happen to interact in the critical window when a rescuer encounters a displaced galgo near the prey-loss terminus.

- **Position:** Galgo requires a dual-prior model: (a) chase displacement prior = greyhound OR=8.34 applies (prey-loss terminus fixation, sighthound-conserved mechanism); (b) approach-avoidance prior = galgo-specific, flight-dominant, not freeze-dominant, requiring passive recovery protocols with zero direct approach attempts for traumatized individuals.

- **Confidence:** high for the dual-prior split (>85%); medium for the specific OR=8.34 point estimate applied to galgos specifically (50–70%), because the CI is wide (1.72–42.38) and galgo-specific empirical chase data does not exist. The directionality (strong last-known-location fixation) is high-confidence; the magnitude (exactly 8.34x) is medium-confidence.

- **Boundary conditions:** The chase-prior transfer applies to galgos that were triggered by prey (rabbit, bird, cat) or fast-moving vehicle stimulus in open terrain. It does not apply to galgos that escaped from home due to fear (fireworks, storm, abuse) — those individuals are governed exclusively by the fear-avoidance system and will behave as traumatized flight animals. Algarve field context makes prey-triggered displacement the dominant scenario (rural terrain, rabbit-dense maquis), so the chase prior applies to the majority of the Algarve galgo lost-dog population. The approach-avoidance prior applies universally to all rescued galgos regardless of trigger type, because the trauma baseline is a population-level feature (Normando 2024: >1 in 5 galgos, p=0.009).

- **What would change this position:** A direct empirical study of galgo (not English greyhound) prey-fixation behavior at prey-loss terminus with a different OR (e.g., significantly lower, <3.0) would weaken the chase prior transfer. If a study showed that Algarve galgos' trauma histories were significantly lower than Spanish rescue galgos (Normando's sample) — e.g., because Algarve galgos are more often pet-abandoned than hunting-discarded — the approach-avoidance distinction would be smaller. Neither adjustment currently has supporting evidence; the trauma documentation is consistent across all galgo rescue orgs reviewed.

- **Evidence weight:** Chase prior: 1 high-quality peer-reviewed study (Starling 2020, OR=8.34), 1 upstream peer-reviewed pilot (Starling 2020 pilot, N=525), phylogenetic reasoning supported by 2 predatory motor sequence sources. Approach-avoidance prior: 1 peer-reviewed empirical study (Normando 2024, n=198 galgos, p=0.009), 1 adoption study (Normando 2021, n=191 galgos), practitioner documentation from 3 rescue orgs (SOS Galgos 18+ profiles, Galgos del Sol, Galgos del Sol MN). No contradicting empirical evidence found for either component.

## Specific priors Nona should encode

**Chase displacement prior (from greyhound OR=8.34, transfer confirmed):**
- When a galgo is prey-triggered, primary search zone should be a radius centered on the **prey-loss terminus** (where visual/auditory contact with prey was lost), not on the escape point (departure from owner/home). The OR=8.34 effect translates to: in an 8:1 ratio, prey-triggered sighthounds are near the prey-loss point vs. having dispersed randomly.
- Sound is more salient than visual for chase initiation in sighthounds. In Algarve maquis, rabbit sounds (rustling, movement) may trigger chase before visual acquisition. The prey-loss terminus may differ from the prey-sighting point by 10–50m in dense scrub.
- Chase motivation is appetitive (self-reinforcing). A galgo will sustain displacement without catching anything. Do not reduce the search radius based on elapsed time alone.

**Approach-avoidance prior (galgo-specific, do not use greyhound data):**
- Rescued galgo: assume flight-dominant fear response to human approach. Direct approach by any human — including owner — will likely trigger further displacement. Use passive recovery protocol (food station + camera trap, no direct pursuit).
- Minimum passive observation period before any active approach: 72 hours of camera-trap sighting without direct human contact.
- Conspecific attraction is the primary lure mechanism. SOS Galgos documents universal dog sociability ("galgos do not like to be on their own") that is not negated by trauma. A calm familiar dog at a feeding station outperforms food alone for traumatized galgos.
- Freeze response (as documented in racing greyhounds) should NOT be expected. A galgo that appears "frozen" in field observations is more likely in the stalk phase of the predatory sequence (assessing movement) than in stress-freeze — do not approach thinking it is catchable.
- Timeline: weeks-to-months approach-avoidance is the documented baseline (Galgos del Sol; SOS Galgos profiles), not days. Recovery plans should be scoped for 4–12 week passive operations in worst-case trauma scenarios.

## Open questions

- No direct empirical data on galgo español prey-fixation behavior exists. The OR=8.34 transfer is a phylogenetic inference. A field study using GPS-tagged galgos in a coursing context would directly test the prey-terminus fixation hypothesis.
- The Normando 2024 comparison group ("other rescue dogs") is not broken down by breed type. If other sighthounds or greyhounds were included in that group, the galgo-vs-greyhound distinction would be even cleaner; if not, the generalization from "other rescue dogs" to "English greyhounds" is an inference, not a measured comparison.
- No data on Algarve-specific galgo population trauma histories. The Algarve galgo population likely includes both hunting-discarded individuals (high trauma) and pet-abandoned individuals (lower trauma). The behavioral prior should ideally be weighted by intake type.
- Normando 2025 "Behavioural characteristics of sighthounds: an exploratory investigation" (Dog Behavior vol 11 no 1) was cited as a companion paper but could not be accessed within budget — this paper may contain more refined galgo vs. greyhound distinctions.
