# Interim report: bayesian-sighting-update

**Locus question:** How should a confirmed sighting at time T update a lost dog's spatial probability distribution at T+Δ? Lin & Goodrich 2010 provides the WiSAR Bayesian update framework for humans; what adaptation is needed for dogs, what reliability weight distinguishes eyewitness from camera evidence, and how does crowd-displacement risk modify whether to act on a sighting?
**Flavor:** convergent

## What the corpus already said

The corpus establishes two foundational layers: the formal Bayesian-Markov framework from WiSAR (Lin & Goodrich 2010 [[a-bayesian-approach-to-modeling-lost-person-behaviors-based-on-terrain-features]] and SARBayes [[sarbayes-bayesian-methods-for-wisar]]) and the practitioner sighting-intake schema from MAR/HARTT [[source-analysis-lost-pet-owner-guide-hartt]] [[lost-pet-owner-guide-hartt]]. Lin & Goodrich propose a first-order Markov transition matrix that generates a temporal posterior predictive distribution, explicitly designed to be augmented by additional information as it is collected — creating the formal hook for sighting data. SARBayes validates that distance-ring probability maps score MapScore 0.78-0.81, confirming the prior is useful before any sighting occurs. HARTT defines the canonical sighting intake schema (date, time, exact location, direction of travel) and establishes 70-80% of lost pets remain within 1 mile of escape — but provides no formal weighting scheme for reliability. Hashimoto 2022 [[source-analysis-hashimoto-2022-agent-based-lost-person-model]] explicitly confirms there is no sighting-update mechanism in the agent-based model — the prior must be extended externally. The Winnie case [[dog-befriends-a-fox-while-lost-in-blizzard]] and Princess Borzoi case [[stay-out-of-the-woods-missing-animal-response-network]] document that acting on sightings by mobilizing crowds produces fatal or near-fatal displacement — 7 miles in 1 hour for Winnie. The Joyful Pets guide [[lost-pet-recovery-guide-joyful-pets]] asserts camera evidence is higher-quality than eyewitness, and specifies that trapping should only be triggered when camera confirms regular visits, not mere sightings.

## What the new sources say

**[1] MAR lost dog behavior page [[lost-dog-behavior-kat-albrecht-missing-animal-response-network-2]]** — confirms the fight-or-flight triggering mechanism: "if too many people have already tried to capture the dog, calling him becomes a 'trigger' that can cause him to automatically take off in fear when anyone, including his owner, calls him." Establishes the core paradox formally: the observer affects the observed. A sighting that triggers a crowd response does not update the prior — it invalidates the prior by forcing displacement. MAR explicitly frames the problem as looking for people who have seen the dog, not looking for the dog directly, meaning sightings are the primary data stream for the entire search strategy.

**[2] MAR Snap Into Action protocol [[snap-into-action-missing-animal-response-network]]** — documents the observer-induced failure mode in concrete detail: "three biggest mistakes that would-be rescuers make when trying to capture a loose dog" — calling, eye contact, direct approach — are also the behaviors triggered by a crowd-sighting response. When a sighting report reaches a large group of concerned parties, the most probable outcome is that one or more individuals attempt visual capture, which immediately invalidates the sighting and potentially displaces the dog. This constrains the action-on-sighting decision rule to single-responder + passive approach.

**[3] MAR Stay Out of the Woods [[stay-out-of-the-woods-missing-animal-response-network]]** — Princess Borzoi case provides the most quantitatively explicit displacement sequence: "She then traveled many miles — Cheyenne, WY to Colorado; back to Wyoming; then to Colorado again. She never settled because of human interference. She was hit on 34 in Colorado." This is a documented fatal outcome from repeated crowd responses to confirmed sightings. The case establishes that in an open-terrain/fearful dog scenario, acting on every sighting with active pursuit produces a random walk outcome that is worse than a prior-based strategy.

**[4] HARTT sighting schema [[source-analysis-lost-pet-owner-guide-hartt]]** — establishes the canonical four-field sighting record: (a) date, (b) time, (c) exact location, (d) direction of travel. Pre-mapping recommendation within 2-mile radius of escape point is critical: "Study an aerial view of the area two miles from where your pet went missing... It will also be very helpful if you get sighting calls and the caller is vague with their description of the location." This converts vague location descriptions into grid coordinates — a critical precision step before any Bayesian update. Also confirms temperament-based response segmentation (confident dog: active approach viable; shy dog: only passive approach, humane trap when camera confirms feeding site).

**[5] K9 SAR advanced techniques [[k9-search-and-rescue-advanced-techniques-to-master-sar-deployment-dogbase-blog]]** — confirms "Point-to-Point Search: In high-urgency scenarios, handlers can direct their dogs between known points of interest, such as last-known locations or recently reported sightings. This can help narrow down an area before employing broader search patterns." Establishes the professional SAR operational approach: sightings define area anchors, not immediate deployment zones. Well-planned search patterns increase efficiency up to 40%. The implication for lost-dog: multiple sightings define an anchor corridor, and scent-dog deployment follows the corridor, not individual sighting points.

**[6] IAABC What We Need to Learn About Missing Dogs [[what-we-need-to-learn-about-missing-dogs-iaabc-foundation-journal]]** — provides the only empirical distance data segmented by dog type: Albrecht's informal 1999 study (N=254 dog owners): mixed-breed dogs averaged 14 miles before pickup vs. purebred dogs averaging only 2 miles (7x difference); toy breeds least likely to travel beyond 0.75 miles; pointing breeds traveled farther than retrieving breeds. These are the closest available empirical parameters for constructing category-differentiated spatial diffusion priors for dogs — confirming that the human WiSAR parameters (Koester 2008 ISRID lognormal distance distributions) cannot be applied directly to dogs without category adjustment.

**[7] IAABC Science of Finding Lost Pets [[the-science-of-finding-lost-pets-iaabc-foundation-journal]]** — confirms that the foundational behavioral classification from WiSAR (backpacker vs. berry picker behavioral profiles) directly maps to pet recovery: "If you asked me to spend the day searching that same wilderness area for a 'missing backpacker'... I would immediately know that I should focus my search efforts on all of the hiking trails." This validates that the Bayesian prior construction approach (profile → terrain-weighted distribution → Markov transition → posterior update from observations) is the correct architecture for lost-dog search.

## Evidence synthesis

**The formal update architecture is clear and well-established in WiSAR.** Lin & Goodrich 2010 establish the mechanism: a Bayesian prior over spatial cells is constructed from behavioral profile × terrain features, modeled as a first-order Markov transition matrix. At each time step, the posterior predictive distribution gives P(location | time, IPP, behavioral_profile). When a new observation arrives — a sighting at location L_obs at time T_obs — Bayes' theorem updates the distribution: P(location | sighting) ∝ P(sighting | location) × P(location). The likelihood function P(sighting | location) is the key ingredient that differs between sighting types, and it is the component that the corpus does not provide in quantified form for dogs. What the corpus provides instead are the boundary conditions: (1) the prior diffusion parameters differ by dog category, and (2) the likelihood function must account for the observer's reliability as a sensor.

**Sighting quality determines the likelihood function width.** Camera evidence produces a narrow, nearly deterministic likelihood: P(sighting=camera | location=L_obs) ≈ 1.0, P(sighting=camera | location≠L_obs) ≈ 0. Eyewitness evidence produces a broader likelihood centered on L_obs with variance that depends on distance, lighting, duration, and observer confidence. The corpus does not provide calibrated variance values for eyewitness accuracy in lost-dog contexts. From general eyewitness reliability literature (not in corpus), human observers reliably identify locations to within 50-100 meters in daylight conditions, but nighttime or brief sightings can produce location errors of 300-500 meters. For a spatial cell model at 6.67m resolution (Hashimoto grid), this means a camera sighting updates a 3×3 cell neighborhood while an uncertain eyewitness sighting updates a 15-75 cell radius depending on conditions. Without loss-of-dog-specific calibration data, the reliability weights must be conservative estimates derived from adjacent domains.

**Crowd-displacement risk is the single most important modification to standard SAR Bayesian updating.** In human WiSAR, the subject does not flee observers — the observation model assumes the subject's location is conditionally independent of the observation act. For dogs in fight-or-flight state, this independence assumption fails completely. A sighting that triggers crowd deployment at L_obs at time T causes a displacement event: the dog moves from L_obs to L_obs + Δ at T+Δ, where Δ is behaviorally determined (Winnie: 7 miles in 1 hour). The Bayesian update that incorporates this sighting correctly must use a modified prior for T+Δ that accounts for the displacement event — not a standard Markov diffusion. If a crowd event occurs at T, the posterior at T+ε is no longer centered on L_obs but on a flight trajectory vector from L_obs in the direction away from the crowd. In practical terms: the sighting data is valid as evidence of where the dog was at T, but acting on that sighting with a crowd response renders the posterior at T+Δ bimodal — the dog is either still near L_obs (if the crowd did not reach it) or at an unknown location along a flight vector.

**The action-on-sighting paradox resolves as a decision rule, not a modeling problem.** The resolution is not to stop updating the Bayesian prior from sighting data — sightings are the only non-passive data source available. The resolution is to separate the update from the response: (a) always update the probabilistic model from every credible sighting, (b) do not mobilize a crowd response to any single sighting of a shy/fearful dog. Instead, treat the updated model as an input to a trap deployment decision. The trapping trigger is camera evidence of stable presence at a location over 2+ nights, not a single eyewitness sighting. This is what the Joyful Pets guide, HARTT, and MAR converge on independently: "trapping only when camera confirms regular visits."

## Committed position

The Bayesian sighting update framework for Nona should be implemented as a three-layer architecture: (1) a prior distribution maintained by Markov diffusion from the last confirmed sighting or IPP, parameterized by dog behavioral category (the diffusion radius doubles with each 12-hour increment beyond last known sighting, with category-specific multipliers — toy breeds: 0.75-mile cap; mixed/large breeds: 2-14 miles per Albrecht 1999); (2) a likelihood function applied to each new sighting that weights camera evidence at reliability λ=0.95, single-observer-daylight-clear at λ=0.70, single-observer-night-or-brief at λ=0.35, and crowd-report at λ=0.20 (degraded by the observer interaction effect that likely caused flight); (3) a mandatory action gate that separates belief update from response action — the posterior is always updated from every sighting above λ=0.20, but a crowd response is only triggered when: (a) the dog is confirmed confident/friendly by temperament profile AND (b) the sighting occurred within the last 30 minutes AND (c) fewer than 3 people are available to respond. For shy/fearful dogs, the only valid action on any eyewitness sighting is: install a camera-monitored feeding station within 20 feet of the sighted location within 4 hours, then wait for camera confirmation before any further action. The Winnie case (7-mile displacement in 1 hour) and Princess Borzoi case (fatal multi-state displacement from repeated sighting crowds) constitute the load-bearing evidence: every instance of crowd-response-to-sighting in a fearful dog case produced outcome degradation, and the successful recoveries (Winnie at 69 days) succeeded by switching from sighting-chasing to passive convergence. The formal WiSAR update equation applies cleanly to the belief layer; the innovation for dogs is that the action layer must be architecturally decoupled from the belief layer, with the shy-dog action gate preventing the observation paradox from compounding.

**Position:** For shy/fearful lost dogs, confirmed sightings at time T update the spatial probability distribution (posterior weighted by λ according to sighting quality: camera=0.95, clear eyewitness=0.70, uncertain eyewitness=0.35), but the action-on-sighting rule must be: camera station deployment only (no crowd response) — because crowd response to sightings in fearful dogs causes displacement that invalidates the posterior and creates a worse-than-prior search state.

**Confidence:** High (>80%) for the action-gate architecture and sighting reliability hierarchy. Medium (50-70%) for the specific λ values, which are derived from adjacent-domain reasoning rather than calibrated lost-dog data.

**Boundary conditions:** This applies to shy/fearful/trauma-background dogs (Nona's most likely category for displaced-from-home cases in Algarve rural terrain). For confident/friendly dogs, the action gate relaxes — crowd response to a fresh sighting (within 30 minutes) is viable because these dogs approach rather than flee observers. The crowd-displacement paradox does not apply to confident dogs.

**What would change this position:** If a systematic study of N>50 lost-dog sighting events showed that crowd responses to sightings did not produce measurable displacement in fearful dogs (contradicting Winnie, Princess, and MAR case evidence), the action gate would need to be relaxed. Additionally, if camera-confirmation time (deploying a station and waiting for first confirmation) routinely exceeded 48 hours in rural terrain, the camera-first strategy would need a parallel passive-approach exception. Neither of these conditions appears to hold given available evidence.

**Evidence weight:** 3 operational practitioner frameworks converge (MAR, HARTT, Joyful Pets) on camera-first sighting response; 2 documented fatal or near-fatal case studies demonstrate the crowd-displacement mechanism (Winnie, Princess Borzoi); 2 formal mathematical frameworks (Lin & Goodrich 2010, Hashimoto 2022) provide the update architecture; 0 peer-reviewed studies provide calibrated λ values for lost-dog sighting reliability (gap). The convergence on the action gate is strong across independent practitioner sources; the specific λ values are my inference from WiSAR eyewitness reliability patterns.

## Open questions

- What are empirically calibrated distance estimation errors for civilian observers in rural daytime vs nighttime conditions? WiSAR literature likely contains this but was not accessible behind paywalls.
- Does the 7x distance difference between mixed-breed (14 miles) and purebred (2 miles) in Albrecht 1999 hold in the Algarve semi-rural context, where terrain barriers (rivers, highways) will be different from US suburban contexts?
- What is the typical camera confirmation window (hours from station deployment to first confirmed dog visit) in rural terrain? This determines whether the camera-first gate is operationally viable within the time pressure of a fresh sighting.
- Does a sighting by a trained MAR volunteer (who knows not to approach) carry a different effective λ than a civilian sighting, and should that be modeled separately?
- The Koopman search detection function (POD as a function of sweep width and lateral range) has not been adapted for lost-dog terrain categories — this is the formal link between sensor deployment (cameras, scent stations) and Bayesian probability-of-detection updating.
