## Thesis / Central claim

A frightened lost dog's behavior is governed primarily by its temperament (confident/social vs shy/skittish), health status, and familiarity with the lost area — not by breed or random chance. HARTT's protocol treats approximately 70–80% of lost pets as remaining within 1 mile of the escape point, concentrates search resources in that radius first, then updates search geometry as confirmed sightings arrive. Every sighting must record four fields (date, time, exact location, direction of travel) plus the sighter's contact information, enabling downstream triangulation of probable movement corridors.

## Methodology / Basis of claims

This is a practitioner guide from HARTT (Arizona), a lost-pet recovery nonprofit. Claims derive from operational case experience — not a published dataset, controlled study, or formal Bayesian model. The 70% / 80% within-1-mile figure (stated as both values in two separate sections of the same document — an internal inconsistency) is presented as HARTT's working heuristic from aggregate case history, not a cited statistic. No N is given, no confidence interval, no source study. All behavioral guidance is case-based inference, not peer-reviewed. The document functions as a structured checklist and intake protocol rather than a research paper.

## Key findings / Claims (with specific numbers where present)

1. **70–80% within-1-mile prior (dual statement, inconsistent).** The guide states "approximately 70% of the lost pets we help stay within 1 mile of where they went missing" (web version, line ~48) and separately "approximately 80% of the lost pets we help stay within 1 mile of where they went missing" (print form version, line ~196). Both versions apply even when the escape location is hundreds of miles from the pet's home address. This is the foundational spatial prior for Bayesian position distribution at Day 0.

2. **Canonical sighting data schema — four required fields.** Every sighting must capture: (a) date, (b) time, (c) exact location, (d) direction of travel. Additionally, the sighter's name and phone number must be retained. This is stated twice verbatim, once in the web version and once in the print form, with identical fields — indicating it is the firm HARTT intake standard, not a suggestion.

3. **Search resource concentration follows prior, then updates on sightings.** "Using that probability, we will concentrate on this area first." The 1-mile radius is the initial search zone. The guide implies (but does not make explicit) that confirmed sightings shift where resources concentrate — the GIS map reference and the instruction to know the area before sighting calls come in supports this inference.

4. **Large-group active searches are contraindicated for frightened dogs.** "HARTT in general discourages rigorous ground searches with large teams because their presence may cause a frightened dog to flee the area." Exception: if the dog is known sick, injured, or geriatric, a quiet grid search is advised. This is a critical behavioral finding: active searches can corrupt sighting data by displacing the dog from its current location.

5. **Calling the pet's name by multiple people triggers flight.** "If 5 different people are calling 'Fluffy!' in 5 different areas or on 5 different streets, the pet will have no idea which direction they are supposed to go. Also, calling a pet's name, clapping or whistling can also trigger a panicked 'Oh no — they're looking for me!' flight response." Only the bonded owner should call the dog's name; for shy dogs, even this may be counterproductive.

6. **Shy dog protocol — capture approach.** Once a shy/skittish dog is located, the owner sits down, angles slightly away from the dog, speaks softly, and waits for the dog to approach. Any pursuit attempt causes the dog to flee (a human cannot outrun a dog). This is directly relevant to sighting-to-action protocol: when a sighting of a shy dog is confirmed, the response must be passive convergence, not active pursuit.

7. **Temperament segmentation determines strategy.** Confident/social dogs: active calling, direct approach can work. Shy/skittish dogs: humane trapping is likely required; the dog probably will not self-return or approach searchers. Medical status (young/healthy vs old/sick/medically compromised) is the second axis — sick or injured dogs warrant immediate quiet grid search regardless of temperament.

8. **Humane trapping trigger: trail camera evidence of feeding.** The guide specifies that humane trapping is indicated when a shy dog is confirmed eating at a specific location via trail camera. This defines the evidentiary threshold — camera confirmation of a feeding location, not just a visual sighting.

9. **Scent-station placement for spatial attraction.** Feeding station plus scent items (owner's clothing, pet waste from bonded pets) placed at the escape point or known proximity area. The scent station functions as a passive spatial attractor, complementary to active search — relevant to Bayesian search theory (the station increases the probability of detection at a known point without displacing the animal).

10. **Flyer density recommendation as information network.** Minimum 100 flyers within ½-mile radius of escape point within 24 hours for dogs (30–40 flyers in 2–3 block radius for cats). The explicit logic: "4 of you looking for your pet; if you put up 100 flyers and each one is seen by 10 people, that will be 1,000 people helping to look for your pet." This is a sighting-generation strategy — maximizing the human sensor network that produces sightings for Bayesian updating.

11. **Drone use caveated.** Drones may help but: most lack night vision (dogs most active after dark); a drone flying too low once the pet is spotted "could spook the animal from the area." Flying too low near a located dog is classified as a corrupting action equivalent to pursuit.

12. **GIS/mapping pre-work before sighting calls.** "Study an aerial view of the area two miles from where your pet went missing. Search for canals, walking paths, wooded areas, other bodies of water, and other possible hiding places. It will also be very helpful if you get sighting calls and the caller is vague with their description of the location." Pre-mapping translates vague sighter descriptions into precise geo-coordinates, increasing the precision of each sighting update.

## Load-bearing citations / sources this source depends on

None cited. The guide is self-contained practitioner experience. No academic references, no named studies, no dataset attribution. The 70–80% figure is HARTT's operational heuristic with no upstream citation. The only external references are operational tools: Maricopa County GIS map (https://gis.maricopa.gov/ACC/Stray/index.html), Petco Love Lost Stray Map, 24PetConnect.com, Craigslist, Nextdoor, Neighbors by Ring, Facebook lost pet groups.

This means: if the pipeline needs a peer-reviewed source for the within-1-mile figure or formal Bayesian sighting models, this source cannot provide it. A depth investigation should target Kat Albrecht's Missing Animal Response Network (MARN), the Lost Pet Research & Recovery (LPRR) studies, or search-and-rescue SAR Bayesian literature.

## Caveats, limitations, contradictions

1. **70% vs 80% internal contradiction.** The two versions of the same guide state different percentages. This is unexplained and suggests the figure is a rough heuristic that varies by author/editor, not an empirically pinned statistic.

2. **No N, no methodology for the heuristic.** The within-1-mile claim has no case count, time period, geographic scope, or breed/size breakdown. It cannot be cited as quantitative evidence without that attribution.

3. **Arizona / Maricopa County context.** The guide is written for urban/suburban Arizona desert terrain with specific local resources (MCACC, Arizona Humane Society, Arizona Facebook groups). The behavioral principles likely generalize, but the resource list does not.

4. **No explicit sighting-weighting or reliability criteria.** The guide collects the four sighting fields but does not provide any criteria for evaluating sighting reliability (e.g., how to weight a nighttime sighting vs daytime, a confident identification vs uncertain). It also does not specify how multiple sightings are combined — there is no triangulation algorithm, just the implicit assumption that the coordinator reviews all sightings together.

5. **Shy dog protocol is operational, not probabilistic.** The guide prescribes the approach behavior correctly but does not give any probability estimate for success rates of passive approach vs trapping, or for how quickly a shy dog relocates after a disturbing stimulus.

6. **Bulk of document is form boilerplate.** Approximately 7,500 of the 11,048 words are web form elements (country dropdowns, intake forms, adoption agreement checkboxes). The substantive recovery guidance is ~3,500 words. Word count should not be taken as an indicator of content density.

## Extracted quotes

> "Even if your pet was lost from an unfamiliar area, approximately 70% of the lost pets we help stay within 1 mile of where they went missing (even if this location is hundreds of miles from their home). Using that probability, we will concentrate on this area first."

The foundational spatial prior. Note: the print form version of the same guide says 80%, not 70% — an unresolved internal inconsistency.

> "Have there been any sightings? We will need to know the date, time, exact location, and direction of travel. Please also be sure to keep the name and phone number of the person who called in each sighting."

The canonical four-field sighting intake schema, stated identically in both the web and print versions of the guide.

> "HARTT in general discourages rigorous ground searches with large teams because their presence may cause a frightened dog to flee the area. However, if there is any belief or indication that the animal may be sick or injured (ie, dog is geriatric, disabled, was seen hit by a car or was in an auto accident), then a careful, quiet grid search is highly advised in case the dog is not able to move."

The anti-active-search principle and its exception — critical for post-sighting response protocols.

> "Do NOT have a multitude of people calling out the pet's name – if 5 different people are calling 'Fluffy!' in 5 different areas or on 5 different streets, the pet will have no idea which direction they are supposed to go. Also, calling a pet's name, clapping or whistling can also trigger a panicked 'Oh no – they're looking for me!' flight response in a pet and can be counterproductive."

Establishes that caller-noise corrupts both the dog's navigation and its stationary behavior — a mechanism for why active searches displace animals from their last-known position.

> "Once on scene, we recommend that the owner sit down, slightly angled away from the pet. The owner should begin talking softly just so the pet can hear their voice and identify them as familiar. Whenever possible, allow the pet to approach the owner. Try not to make any rapid movement or gestures."

The shy dog protocol: passive approach posture, voice identification, no pursuit. Defines the action protocol when a confirmed sighting triggers an owner response.

> "It will also be very helpful if you get sighting calls and the caller is vague with their description of the location they saw your pet. Knowing the area ahead of time will expedite your response time."

The case for pre-mapping: converts vague sighter geography into precise coordinates before sightings arrive, increasing each sighting's positional precision.
