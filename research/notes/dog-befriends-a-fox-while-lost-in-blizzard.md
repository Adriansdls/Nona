Winnie recovered after 69 days

Many lost pet recovery teams spend considerable amounts of money, time, and efforts to recover skittish, hard-to-catch dogs. The story of Winnie demonstrates the dedication and collaborative effort with several rescuers in Silver Spring, Maryland over the past few months to capture one dog.
The story below was crafted by JoAnne Desjardins, a volunteer with Tailed Treasures of Maryland, one of many teams working in blizzard conditions to recover skittish, hard-to-catch dogs. Whereas many rescue teams unfortunately work
against
each other, this story is a beautiful example of collaboration and how companion animal lives can be saved when various resources work together!

The Maryland winter this year has been one of the worst in decades, but for a dog like Winnie, the brutal cold was secondary to her fear. Winnie, a beautiful soul originally rescued from the trauma of a puppy breeding facility, went missing from a pet sitter’s home in Silver Spring, Maryland during the Thanksgiving holiday. For Winnie, humans had historically been a source of confinement and commerce, not comfort. When she vanished, she wasn’t just a lost pet; she was a dog reverting to survival mode to escape the life she had once known—one of crates, puppy factories, and a profound lack of human kindness.
A Partnership of Hope
The search was a deeply personal mission led by our team at Tailed Treasures of MD in coordination with our dear friend Liza Kudel, who runs A Home of Their Own Rescue -the very rescue Winnie had been adopted from. Having her original rescuer involved provided the heart and history needed to keep going through the darkest of days.
The journey began with a heart-stopping chase. Winnie first appeared on Layhill Road. The well-meaning crowd of people trying to “help” Winnie actually triggered her flight instinct. In a single hour, Winnie bolted and ran 7 miles from her escape point to Leisure World. Eventually, she settled on a beautiful property bordered by quiet farms. It was a sanctuary, of sorts, for a dog who feared four walls, but it required a massive logistical effort to manage.
John, a cornerstone of our team, worked tirelessly moving and resetting our heavy traps whenever Winnie shifted her patterns. His willingness to handle the grueling physical labor of the setup ensured we always had a chance, no matter where she moved.
The Compassion of Strangers
This rescue simply would not have been possible without the incredible compassion of the homeowners who essentially allowed us to take over their property to get Winnie safe. Their kindness went far beyond just permission; during the most brutal parts of the snowstorm, when the roads were impassable, they stepped in to keep Winnie fed until our team could finally dig out and reach her again. Their partnership was the lifeline Winnie needed to survive.
Fighting the Elements and the Cost of Care
Winnie continued to refuse to enter our enclosure trap, known as “The Missy Trap.” This was likely due to her past trauma with confinement. Because of this, we had to get creative to keep her alive as the weather turned deadly. Before the big snowstorm hit, our team worked to build custom shelters for her on the property where she was hanging around. We hoped that she would use these for warmth since she wouldn’t step foot inside our humane traps.
This case was also one of the most expensive we’ve ever had. It was a daily struggle to ensure that Winnie got enough food to keep her on the property. Because we were also feeding the foxes on-site to minimize conflict, the sheer amount of food we had to purchase was staggering! We had to ensure that Winnie ate enough to maintain her body heat against a record-breaking winter, doing everything in our power to provide a safety net for a dog who wasn’t yet ready to be caught.
The Invisible Bridge
While Kim and Natasha were the face of the rescue on-site, a monumental effort was happening in the shadows. Jill Barsky worked tirelessly behind the scenes, her phone never silent as she contacted experts and searched for alternative, specialized methods to safely capture a dog as traumatized and trap-shy as Winnie was.
It is important to highlight that this success did not happen overnight; many, many weeks of invisible work took place before Winnie was even remotely ready for this day. There were endless strategy meetings, phone calls, and agonizing shifts in tactics. Every day that passed was a calculated move by a team that refused to give up on a dog who had already been failed by humans for most of her life.
Survival and the Foxes
For many months, Kim Johnson and Natasha Keogh became her silent guardians. They braved some of the most historic snowstorms and freezing temperatures Maryland has seen in decades, working opposite shifts—Kim in the afternoons and Natasha in the early mornings or late evenings. Through their constant presence, they slowly began to build a “routine” of safety, using calming signals and consistent feeding to reach through Winnie’s wall of fear.
We brought in Ryan from Arkcan Drone Services, watching Winnie through a thermal lens for 16 hours straight. We watched her fiercely patrol her food, chasing off foxes to claim her territory. Yet, in a beautiful twist of nature, she eventually made peace with one particular fox. The two were seen sleeping near each other—two creatures finding warmth in a shared struggle against a record-breaking winter.
The Miracle of Phoebe
After over two months of emotional exhaustion, balancing full-time jobs and other rescue cases, our team was at a breaking point. We had reached into our toolkit many times, even bringing other dogs out to the property in hopes of coaxing her in, but Winnie showed no interest. She remained distant, her instincts still rooted in fear.
Then came the “Golden Ticket.” Liza had a new rescue doodle named Phoebe at A Home of Their Own (the same rescue Winnie was adopted from). On a whim of hope, we brought her out. The transformation was instant. Where other dogs had failed to spark a connection with Winnie, Phoebe was the key! The dog who had outmaneuvered every trap we had set and feared every technique we had tried began to follow Phoebe with pure, wagging excitement. It was the tireless dedication and months of groundwork by Kim and Natasha that truly brought us to this moment; without the trust they had painstakingly built day after day, Winnie would never have stayed calm enough for this meeting.
Phoebe (in harness) helped capture Winnie (right)
As Phoebe arrived, Kim Johnson was right there on the ground, working skillfully to keep Winnie calm and lure her closer to Liza and Phoebe in hopes of finally securing her. Because of Kim’s steady presence and the trust she had established, Winnie followed Phoebe right toward the parked cars. With the car doors open and 69 days of adrenaline and love behind her, Liza moved—securing Winnie with a slip lead and pulling her into the safety of the car.
Home at Last
Winnie is finally safe. She survived the snow, the foxes, and 69 days of wandering in one of the harshest winters on record. She is now warm, secure, and decompressing with an incredible foster family and another foster dog who is helping her learn how to be a pet again.
Winnie resting at home after her adventure!
To our entire team at Tailed Treasures
There are no words for the gratitude I feel. Every single member of this team played a vital part in this rescue. Whether you were out in the snow building shelters, moving traps, trying calming signals daily, monitoring cameras, or providing the emotional support we needed to keep going—this victory belongs to
all
of us.
We are sobbing as we write this because we poured our lives into this rescue. We stayed when it was cold, we stayed when she refused us, and we stayed until the miracle happened. Winnie’s story is a testament to the fact that no dog is ever “too far gone”—sometimes, they just need a friend like Phoebe to show them the way, and a team of compassionate, stubborn humans to never stop waiting at the end of the path.”
