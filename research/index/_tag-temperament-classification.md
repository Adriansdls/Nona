Defines 6 factors influencing distance traveled by lost dogs: (1) temperament, (2) circumstances of disappearance, (3) weather, (4) terrain, (5) appearance, (6) population density. Establishes three-category behavioral taxonomy still used by MAR: Gregarious (wiggly-butt, friendly — found close to home, often informally adopted); Aloof (wary, avoid strangers initially, eventually approachable with food/patience, often recovered weeks/months later by experienced rescuers); Xenophobic (fear-driven, will run even from owners, high car-strike risk, require dog traps or calm dogs for capture). Circumstances: dogs escaping to explore a scent meander/double back short distance; dogs bolting from fireworks/thunder run several miles at blind speed. Population density: Manhattan escape → short travel; rural Rocky Mountains → long travel. Identifies owner behavioral failure modes: 'wait-and-see', tunnel vision, grief avoidance. Identifies rescuer failure modes: misinterpreting xenophobic temperament as abuse history, assuming rural loose dog was dumped. Key practical protocol implication: never chase a xenophobic lost dog. `evergreen`
