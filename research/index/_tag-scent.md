Dogs have 220–300 million olfactory receptors (vs. 5–6 million in humans) and an olfactory bulb 40x larger; olfactory turbinates maximize scent-particle contact time. Human scent = skin flakes + sweat (eccrine/apocrine) + sebum + bacterial metabolites — each person has a unique profile detectable even hours or days old. Two primary SAR methods: (1) AIR-SCENTING — nose high, detects airborne scent over large open areas, no fixed start point needed; (2) GROUND TRACKING — nose low, follows footstep odor on surface, most effective from known last-position. Grass and soft soil retain scent far better than concrete or asphalt. Environmental factors: warm-humid conditions preserve scent longest; cold-dry conditions dissipate it fastest; variable wind scatters the trail; rocky/sandy surfaces require air-scenting instead of ground tracking.
