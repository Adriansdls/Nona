- [[holding-back-the-genes-limitations-of-research-into-canine-behavioural-genetics]] — Holding back the genes: limitations of research into canine behavioural genetics - PMC — University of Sydney comprehensive review (van Rooy et al. 2014, Canine Genetics and Epidemiology, DOI: 10.1186/2052-6687-1-7) of methodological limitations in canine behavioral genetics research. Core thesis: behavioral phenotyping challenges, phenotyping inconsistency, and lack of standardized terminology prevent meta-analysis and hold back the field. Key findings: (1) All canine behavior has a genetic component, but behavior is polygenic and gene mapping has been far less successful than for monogenic diseases. (2) Some behaviors are breed-specific and demonstrably heritable — herding/stalking in border collies, livestock guarding in maremmas, flank sucking in Dobermanns, tail chasing in bull terriers — but these are exceptions with strong selection pressure, not the general case. (3) Breed-level behavioral differences in aggression are real but within-breed variance is always high; Swedish DMA on 13,000+ dogs across 31 breeds shows significant between-breed aggression score differences but high within-breed variance simultaneously. (4) C-BARQ study (Duffy et al. via van Rooy): dachshunds, Chihuahuas, Jack Russell terriers show higher serious human-directed aggression; Akitas, Jack Russells, pit bull types show higher dog-directed aggression — but individual variation remains the dominant source of variance. (5) Body size and morphotype correlate with behavior: small dogs more aggressive toward people (inverse relationship statistically significant); short skulls show higher human-attention orientation. (6) Modern breed phenotypes reflect current breeding selection, not historical function — making historical breed-behavior assumptions unreliable. (7) Fear/anxiety phenotyping is particularly problematic: neural pathways for fear vs. anxiety differ (amygdala-initiated vs. cortex-initiated) but are typically conflated in studies. (8) Behavioral problems lead to 10-15% of North American dog/cat euthanasias and cited as leading cause of death in young dogs.
