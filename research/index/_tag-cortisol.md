- [[dont-call-your-dog-missing-animal-response-network]] — Don't Call Your Dog! - Missing Animal Response Network — MAR Network page explaining the physiological and behavioral basis for the 'never call / never chase' rule. Core mechanism: when a dog is in fight-or-flight mode, calling its name by strangers becomes a conditioned trigger for bolting — other people have already called/chased the dog before the owner arrives, so 'calling' is now a fear trigger even for the owner's own voice. Key: olfactory processing shuts down during peak adrenaline state, so even food lures may temporarily fail. Cortisol flooding the brain causes temporary memory loss — owner's face/voice not recognized until calm is restored. Recommended approach: sit/lie flat on ground, avoid direct eye contact, use sideways hip-facing posture (not predator straight-on approach), make lip-smacking 'nummy' sounds, drop food on ground, use crinkly chip bags (dogs associate sound with food), SING (unique sound not used by other failed capturers). Do not whistle, clap, or slap leg — these are triggers used by previous failed capture attempts. Once parasympathetic nervous system re-engages (calming signals activate it), cortisol drops, dog may recognize owner by scent. During full fight-or-flight, olfactory section of brain shuts down — food lures fail at peak panic. `review`

Lost dogs gravitate to woods/cover for safety from perceived threats. Search parties, ATVs, drones, and even foot-traffic on dead leaves/branches can push a dog out of its established shelter and into fatal territory. Cortisol flooding the brain causes sympathetic nervous system to temporarily block memory — dog doesn't recognize owner/guardian until parasympathetic system reengages via calming. Case study: dog pushed out of woods by well-meaning search party, traveled opposite direction, hit by train. Case study (Borzoi 'Princess'): human interference at every sighting caused her to flee from Wyoming to Colorado and back repeatedly until she was hit on highway 34 in Colorado. Protocol: keep area quiet; set food station (smelly canned food) and trail camera 10-20 feet into woods during midday (dogs hunker down midday per Alan Beck 'Ecology of the Stray Dog'). Scent-elevation tip: spray food scent high on tree limbs to carry further. Grill food near home if woods are within a few hundred feet — creates scent cloud. Never set trap until confirmed dog is eating at station on trail camera. `review`
